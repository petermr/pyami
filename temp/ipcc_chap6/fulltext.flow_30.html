6.4.2.2
Wind Energy 1
Wind power is increasingly competitive with other forms of electricity generation and is the low-cost 2
option in many applications (high confidence). Costs have declined by 18% and 40% on land and 3
offshore since 2015 (high confidence), and further reductions can be expected by 2030 (medium 4
confidence). Critical areas for continued improvement are technology advancements and economies of 5
scale (high confidence). Global future potential is primarily limited by onshore land availability in wind 6
power-rich areas, lack of supporting infrastructure, grid integration, and access to finance (especially in 7
developing countries) (high confidence). 8
Energy from wind is abundant, and the estimated technical potentials surpass the total amount of energy 9
needed to limit warming to well below 2°C (high confidence). Recent global estimates of potentially 10
exploitable wind energy resource are in the range of 557–717 PWh yr
-1
(2005–2580 EJ yr
-1
) (Eurek et 11
al. 2017; Bosch et al. 2017, 2018; McKenna et al. 2022), or 20–30 times the 2017 global electricity 12
demand. Studies have suggested that ‘bottom-up’ approaches may overestimate technical potentials 13
(Miller et al. 2015; Kleidon and Miller 2020). But even in the most conservative ‘top-down’ approaches, 14
the technical wind potential surpasses the amount needed to limit warming to well below 2°C (Bosch 15
et al. 2017; Eurek et al. 2017; Volker et al. 2017). The projected climate change mitigation from wind 16
energy by 2100 ranges from 0.3°C–0.8°C depending on the precise socio-economic pathway and wind 17
energy expansion scenario followed (Barthelmie and Pryor 2021). Wind resources are unevenly 18
distributed over the globe and by time of the year (Petersen and Troen 2012), but potential hotspots 19
exist on every continent (Figure 6.10) as expressed by the wind power density (a quantitative measure 20
of wind energy available at any location). Technical potentials for onshore wind power vary 21
considerably, often because of inconsistent assessments of suitability factors (McKenna et al. 2020). 22
The potential for offshore wind power is larger than for onshore because offshore wind is stronger and 23
less variable (Bosch et al. 2018). Offshore wind is more expensive, however, because of higher costs 24
for construction, maintenance, and transmission. Wind power varies at a range of time scales, from 25
annual to sub-seconds; the effects of local short-term variability can be offset by power plant control, 26
flexible grid integration, and storage (Barra et al. 2021) (section 6.4.3). In some regions, interannual 27
variations in wind energy resources could be important for optimal power system design (Wohland et 28
al. 2019a; Coker et al. 2020). 29
