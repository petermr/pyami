Electricity systems require a range of different functional roles – for example, providing energy, 16
capacity, or ancillary services. As a result, a range of different types of generation, energy storage, and 17
transmission resources may be deployed in these systems (Baik et al. 2021). There are many options 18
for each of these roles, each with their strengths and weaknesses (Sections 6.4.3 and 6.4.4), and 19
deployment of these options will be influenced by the evolution of technological costs, system benefits, 20
and local resources (Veers et al. 2019; Mai et al. 2018; Bistline et al. 2018; Hirth 2015; Fell and Linn 21
2013). 22
System management is critical for zero- or negative-emissions electricity systems. Maintaining 23
reliability will increasingly entail system planning and operations that account for characteristics of 24
supply- and demand-side resources (Hu et al. 2018). Coordinated planning and operations will likely 25
become more prevalent across portions of the electricity system (e.g., integrated generation, 26
transmission, and distribution planning), across sectors, and across geographies (Bistline and Young 27
2019; Chan et al. 2018; Konstantelos et al. 2017; EPRI 2017, Section 6.4.3). 28
Energy storage will be increasingly important in net zero energy systems, especially in systems with 29
shares of VRE (high confidence). Deployment of energy storage will vary based on the system benefits 30
and values of different options (Arbabzadeh et al. 2019; Denholm and Mai 2019). Diurnal storage 31
options like lithium-ion batteries have different value than storing and discharging electricity over 32
longer periods through long-duration energy storage with less frequent cycling, which require different 33
technologies, supporting policies, and business models (Sepulveda et al. 2021; Dowling et al. 2020; 34
Gallo et al. 2016; Albertus et al. 2020; Blanco and Faaij 2017) (Section 6.4.4). The value of energy 35
storage varies with the level of deployment and on the competitiveness of economic complements such 36
as VRE options (Bistline and Young 2020; Mileva et al. 2016) and substitutes such as flexible demand 37
(Brown et al. 2018; Merrick et al. 2018), transmission (Merrick et al. 2018; Brown et al. 2018; 38
Schlachtberger et al. 2017; Bistline and Young 2019), trade (Bistline et al. 2020b), dispatchable 39
generators (Hittinger and Lueken 2015; Gils et al. 2017; Arbabzadeh et al. 2019), DAC (Daggash et al. 40
2019), and efficiencies in system operations (Tuohy et al. 2015). 41
