Hydroelectric power plants may pose serious environmental and societal impacts (high confidence) 19
(Mccartney 2009). Dams may lead to fragmentation of ecological habitats because they act as barriers 20
for migration of fish and other land and water-borne fauna, sediments, and water flow. These barriers 21
can be mitigated by sediment passes and fish migration aids, and with provision of environmental flows. 22
Below dams, there can be considerable alterations to vegetation, natural river flows, retention of 23
sediments and nutrients, and water quality and temperature. Construction of large reservoirs leads to 24
loss of land, which may result in social and environmental consequences. Minimizing societal and 25
environmental impacts requires taking into account local physical, environmental, climatological, 26
social, economic, and political aspects during the planning stage (Killingtveit 2020). Moreover, when 27
large areas of land are flooded by dam construction, they generate GHGs (Phyoe and Wang 2019; 28
Maavara et al. 2020; Prairie et al. 2018). On the other hand, hydropower provides flexible, competitive 29
low-emission electricity, local economic benefits (e.g., by increasing irrigation and electricity 30
production in developing countries), and ancillary services such as municipal water supply, irrigation 31
and drought management, navigation and recreation, and flood control (IRENA 2021b). However, the 32
long term economic benefits to communities affected by reservoirs are a subject of debate (de Faria et 33
al. 2017; Catolico et al. 2021). 34
Public support for hydroelectric energy is generally high (Steg 2018), and higher than support for coal, 35
gas, and nuclear. Yet, public support for hydro seems to differ for existing and new projects (high 36
confidence). Public support is generally high for small and medium scale hydropower in regions where 37
hydropower was historically used (Gormally et al. 2014). Additionally, there is high support for existing 38
large hydropower projects in Switzerland (Plum et al. 2019; Rudolf et al. 2014), Canada (Boyd et al. 39
2019), and Norway (Karlstrøm and Ryghaug 2014), where it is a trusted and common energy source. 40
Public support seems lower for new hydropower projects (Hazboun and Boudet 2020), and the 41
construction of new large hydropower plants has been met with strong resistance in some areas 42
(Bronfman et al., 2015; Vince, 2010). People generally perceive hydroelectric energy as clean and a 43
non-contributor to climate change and environmental pollution (Kaldellis et al. 2013). For example, in 44
Sweden, people believed that existing hydropower projects have as few negative environmental impacts 45
as solar, and even less than wind (Ek 2005). However, in areas where the construction of new large-46
scale hydroelectric energy is met with resistance, people believe that electricity generation from hydro 47
can cause environmental, social, and personal risks (Bronfman et al., 2012; Kaldellis et al., 2013). 48
