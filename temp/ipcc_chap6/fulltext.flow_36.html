The construction time of hydroelectric power plants is longer than many other renewable technologies, 1
and that construction time may be extended by the additional time it takes to fill the reservoir. This 2
extended timeline can create uncertainty in the completion of the project. The uncertainty is due to 3
insecurity in year-to-year variations in precipitation and the water inflows required to fill reservoirs. 4
This is especially critical in the case of trans-boundary hydroelectric power plants, where filling up the 5
reservoirs can have large implications on downstream users in other nations. As a result of social and 6
environmental constraints, only a small fraction of potential economic hydropower projects can be 7
developed, especially in developed countries. Many developing countries have major undeveloped 8
hydropower potential, and there are opportunities to develop hydropower combined with other 9
economic activities such as irrigation (Lacombe et al. 2014). Competition for hydropower across 10
country borders can lead to conflict, which could be exacerbated if climate alters rainfall and streamflow 11
(Ito et al. 2016). 12

Nuclear Energy 13
Nuclear power can deliver low-carbon energy at scale (high confidence). Doing so will require 14
improvements in managing construction of reactor designs that hold the promise of lower costs and 15
broader use (medium confidence). At the same time, nuclear power continues to be affected by cost 16
overruns, high up-front investment needs, challenges with final disposal of radioactive waste, and 17
varying public acceptance and political support levels (high confidence). 18
There are sufficient resources for substantially increasing nuclear deployment (medium confidence). 19
Estimates for identified uranium resources have been increasing steadily over the years. Conventional 20
uranium resources have been estimated to be sufficient for over 130 years of supply at current levels of 21
use; 100 years were estimated in 2009 (Hahn 1983; NEA/IAEA 2021). In the case of future uranium 22
resource scarcity, thorium or recycling of spent fuel might be used as alternatives. Interest in these 23
alternatives has waned with better understanding of uranium deposits, their availability, and low prices 24
(OECD NEA 2015; IAEA 2005). 25
There are several possible nuclear technology options for the period from 2030 to 2050 (medium 26
confidence). In addition to electricity, nuclear can also be used to produce low-carbon hydrogen and 27
freshwater (Kayfeci et al. 2019; Kavvadias and Khamis 2014)

Large reactors. The nuclear industry has entered a new phase of reactor construction, based on 29
evolutionary designs. These reactors achieve improvements over previous designs through small to 30
moderate modifications, including improved redundancy, increased application of passive safety 31
features, and significant improvements to containment design to reduce the risk of a major accident 32
(MIT 2018). Examples include European - EPR, Korean - APR1400, U.S. - AP1000, Chinese - 33
HPR1000 or Russian - VVER-1200. 34

Long-term operation (LTO) of the current fleet. Continued production from nuclear power will 35
depend in part on life extensions of the existing fleet. By the end of 2020, two-thirds of nuclear 36
power reactors will have been operational for over 30 years. The design lifetime of most of existing 37
reactors is 30–40 years. Engineering assessments have established that reactors can operate safely 38
for longer if key replaceable components (e.g., steam generator, mechanical and electrical 39
equipment, instrumentation and control parts) are changed or refurbished (IAEA 2018). The first 40
lifetime extension considered in most of the countries typically is 10–20 years (OECD IEA NEA 41
2020). 42
