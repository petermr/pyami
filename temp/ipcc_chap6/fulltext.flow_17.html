Global coal consumption has been declining, with small fluctuations, since it peaked in 2013 (IEA 1
2020d). Coal is faring differently across regions. Coal use has been decreasing in the OECD regions, 2
particularly in the U.S. and the European Union, while remaining mostly flat in China after a period of 3
growth, and it is continuing to increase in other major developing Asian economies (IEA 2020d). Trends 4
in the electricity sector, where most coal is being consumed, are similar. Growth in coal-fired electricity 5
generation capacity in the Asia Pacific region has offset retirements in North America and Europe 6
(Jakob et al. 2020). 7
Reductions in coal consumption have been driven in large part by non-climate factors, most notably 8
environmental regulations to address air pollution, rapidly declining costs of renewables, and lower 9
natural gas prices, especially inexpensive unconventional gas in the U.S. (Diluiso et al 2021; 10
Vinichenko et al., 2021; Culver and Hong 2016). Older coal-fired power plants that cannot meet new 11
environmental regulations or have become unprofitable or uncompetitive have been closed in many 12
regions. Moreover, coal power expansion has slowed down in Asia, as countries have suspended and 13
cancelled new projects for reasons such as overcapacity, environmental constraints, and the 14
development of renewables (see Box 6.2). 15
Different regions have replaced retired coal with different energy sources. Old coal fleets have been 16
replaced approximately half by gas and half by renewables in the U.S., mainly by renewables in the 17
European Union, and by advanced coal plants and renewables in Asia (EMBER 2020). Replacing old 18
coal with new coal facilities is inconsistent with limiting warming to 2ºC or below {high confidence} 19
(Pfeiffer et al. 2016, 2018; Smith et al. 2019; Tong et al. 2019, Section 6.7.4). 20
Major coal consuming countries with abundant coal reserves remain far from phasing out coal (Spencer 21
et al. 2018; Edenhofer et al. 2018). In most developing countries with large coal reserves, coal use has 22
been increasing to support energy security and because it is perceived to have lower costs than 23
alternatives (Steckel et al. 2015; Kalkuhl et al 2019). However, coal faces increasing business risks 24
from the decreasing costs of alternative, low-emissions energy sources and increasing focus on air 25
pollution and other environmental impacts from coal mining and use (Garg et al. 2017; Sovacool et al 26
2021). Continued coal builds, mostly in developing countries, will increase the risks of stranded assets 27
(see Box 6.13) (Farfan Orozco 2017; Saygin et al. 2019; Cui et al. 2019). 28
Economic, social, and employment impacts of accelerated coal phaseouts tend to be significant in coal-29
dependent regions. Tailored reemployment has been used to support coal transitions in some regions. 30
Although some estimates show higher employment opportunities from low-carbon energy (Garrett-31
Peltier 2017), results vary across regions. Moreover, even with a net increase in total employment, in 32
the long run, renewable jobs are often located outside of coal regions and require different skill sets 33
from the coal industry (Spencer et al. 2018). In a broader sense, achieving a “just transition” also 34
requires managing the impacts on regional economic development for coal-dependent communities and 35
the effects of higher energy prices for consumers and energy-intensive industries through a 36
comprehensive policy package (see Box6.2) (Jakob et al. 2020; Green and Gambhir 2020). 37
