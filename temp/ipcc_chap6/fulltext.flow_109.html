Switching to Low-Carbon Energy Carriers. Switching to energy carriers produced from low-carbon 5
sources will be an important strategy for energy sector decarbonization. Accelerated electrification of 6
end uses such as light duty transport, space heating, and cooking is a critical near-term mitigation 7
strategy (Waisman et al. 2019; Sugiyama 2012; Zou et al. 2015; Rockström et al. 2017; IEA 2019f; 8
Tang et al. 2021a). Electricity supplies 48-58% (interquartile range) of the global final energy demand 9
by 2050 in scenarios limiting warming to 1.5°C with no or limited overshoot and 36-47% in likely 10
below 2°C scenarios (Figure 6.29). Globally, the current level of electrification is about 20%. 11
Indirect electrification encompasses the use of electricity to produce hydrogen and synthetic fuels 12
(efuels or power fuels). The extent of indirect electrification of final energy will depend on resource 13
endowments and other regionally specific circumstances. Although indirect electrification is less 14
efficient compared to direct electrification, it allows low-carbon fuels to be imported from regions with 15
abundant low carbon electricity generation resources (Fasihi and Breyer 2020; Fasihi and Bogdanov 16
2016; Lehtveer et al. 2019, Box 6.10 on regional integration). 17
While electrifying end uses is a key decarbonization strategy, some end uses such as long-distance 18
transport (freight, aviation, and shipping) and energy-intensive industries will be harder to electrify. For 19
these sectors, alternative fuels or energy carriers such as biofuels, hydrogen, ammonia or synthetic 20
methane, may be needed (see Section 6.6 and Box 6.9). Most scenarios find that hydrogen consumption 21
will grow gradually, becoming more valuable when the energy system has become predominantly low-22
carbon (Figure 6.31). 23
