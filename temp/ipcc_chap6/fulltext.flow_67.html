Third, social costs and benefits can affect climate action (Farrow et al. 2017), although people do not 8
always recognize this (Nolan et al. 2008; Noppers et al. 2014). People engage more in mitigation actions 9
when they think others expect them to do so and when others act as well (Rai et al. 2016; Harland et al. 10

Fourth, mitigation actions, including saving energy and hot water, limiting meat consumption, and 19
investing in energy efficiency, resource efficiency in buildings, and renewable energy generation are 20
more likely when people more strongly care about others and the environment (Van Der Werff and Steg 21
2015; Steg et al. 2015; Wolske et al. 2017). People across the world generally strongly value the 22
environment (Bouman and Steg 2019; Steg 2016), suggesting that they are motivated to mitigate climate 23
change. The more individuals are aware of the climate impact of their behaviour, the more they think 24
their actions can help reduce such impacts, which strengthens their moral norms to act accordingly, and 25
promotes mitigation actions (Steg and de Groot 2010; Jakovcevic and Steg 2013; Chen 2015; Wolske 26
et al. 2017). 27
Initial mitigation actions can encourage engagement in other mitigation actions when people experience 28
that such actions are easy and effective (Lauren et al. 2016), and when initial actions make them realize 29
they are a pro-environmental person, motivating them to engage in more mitigation actions so as to be 30
consistent (van der Werff et al. 2014; Lacasse 2015, 2016; Peters et al. 2018). This implies it would be 31
important to create conditions that make it likely that initial mitigation actions motivate further actions. 32

Designing feasible, desirable, and cost-effective energy sector mitigation strategies requires comparison 34
between the different mitigation options. One such metric is the cost of delivering one unit of energy, 35
for example, the levelized cost, or USD MWh
-1
, of electricity produced from different sources. LCOEs 36
are useful because they normalize the costs per unit of service provided. While useful in characterizing 37
options in broad strokes, it is important to acknowledge and understand several caveats associated with 38
these metrics, particularly when applied globally. They may be constructed with different discount 39
rates; they require information on energy input costs for options that require energy inputs (e.g., fossil 40
electricity generation, biofuels); they depend on local resource availability, for example solar insolation 41
for solar power, wind classes for wind power, and rainfall and streamflow for hydropower; and actual 42
implementation costs may include additional elements, for example, the costs of managing electricity 43
grids heavily dependent on VRE electricity sources. These complicating factors vary across regions, 44
some depend strongly on the policy environment in which mitigation options are deployed, and some 45
depend on how technologies are constructed and operated. 46
