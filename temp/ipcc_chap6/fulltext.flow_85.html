6.6.2.1
Limited and/or Targeted Use of Fossil Fuels 7
Net-zero energy systems will
use far less fossil fuel than today (high confidence). The precise 8
quantity of fossil fuels will largely depend upon the relative costs of such fuels, electrification, 9
alternative fuels, and CDR (see Section 6.6.2.4) in the energy system (high confidence). All of these are 10
affected by regional differences in resources (McGlade and Ekins 2015), existing energy infrastructure 11
(Tong et al. 2019), demand for energy services, and climate and energy policies. Fossil fuel use may 12
persist, for example, if and where the costs of such fuels and the compensating carbon management 13
(e.g., CDR, CCS) are less than non-fossil energy. For most applications, however, it is likely that 14
electrification (McCollum et al. 2014; Madeddu et al. 2020; Zhang and Fujimori 2020) or use of non-15
fossil alternative fuels (Zeman and Keith 2008; Graves et al. 2011; Hänggi et al. 2019; Ueckerdt et al. 16
2021) will prove to be the cheapest options. Most residual demand for fossil fuels is likely to 17
predominantly be petroleum and natural gas given their high energy density (Davis et al. 2018), while 18
demand for coal in net zero energy systems is likely to be very low (Luderer et al. 2018; Jakob et al. 19
2020, Section 6.7.4) (high confidence). 20
There is considerable flexibility regarding the overall quantity of liquid and gaseous fuels that will be 21
required in net zero energy systems (high confidence) (Figure 6.22, Section 6.7.4). This will be 22
determined by the relative value of such fuels as compared to systems which rely more or less heavily 23
on zero-emissions electricity. In turn, the share of any fuels that are fossil or fossil-derived is uncertain 24
and will depend on the feasibility of CCS and CDR technologies and long-term sequestration as 25
compared to alternative, carbon-neutral fuels. Moreover, to the extent that physical, biological, and/or 26
socio-political factors limit the availability of CDR (Smith et al. 2015; Field and Mach 2017), carbon 27
management efforts may prioritize residual emissions related to land use and other non-energy sources. 28

There are many possible configurations and technologies for zero- or net-negative-emissions electricity 33
systems (high confidence). These systems could entail a mix of variable renewables, dispatchable 34
renewables (e.g., biomass, hydropower), other firm, dispatchable (“on-demand”) low-carbon generation 35
(e.g., nuclear, CCS-equipped capacity), energy storage, transmission, carbon removal options (e.g., 36
BECCS, DACCS), and demand management (Bistline and Blanford 2021b; Bistline et al. 2018; Jenkins 37
et al. 2018b; Luderer et al. 2017). The marginal cost of deploying electricity sector mitigation options 38
increases as electricity emissions approach zero; in addition, the most cost-effective mix of system 39
resources changes as emissions approach zero and, therefore, so do the implications of electricity sector 40
mitigation for sustainability and other societal goals (Cole et al. 2021; Jayadev et al. 2020; Bistline et 41
al. 2018; Mileva et al. 2016; Sepulveda et al. 2018). Key factors influencing the electricity mix include 42
relative costs and system benefits, local resource bases, infrastructure availability, regional integration 43
