Various factors shape whether such mitigation actions are feasible and considered by end users, 18
including contextual factors, individual abilities, and motivational factors. Mitigation actions can be 19
facilitated and encouraged by targeting relevant barriers and enablers (section 6.7.6.2). 20
Contextual factors, such as physical and climate conditions, infrastructure, available technology, 21
regulations, institutions, culture, and financial conditions define the costs and benefits of mitigation 22
options that enable or inhibit their adoption (high confidence). Geographic location and climate factors 23
may make some technologies, such as solar PV or solar water heaters, impractical (Chang et al. 2009). 24
Culture can inhibit efficient use of home heating or PV (Sovacool and Griffiths 2020), low carbon diets 25
(Dubois et al. 2019), and advanced fuel choices (Van Der Kroon et al. 2013). Also, favourable financial 26
conditions promote the uptake of PV (Wolske and Stern 2018), good facilities increase recycling 27
(Geiger et al. 2019), and vegetarian meal sales increase when more vegetarian options are offered.. 28
Mitigation actions are more likely when individuals feel capable to adopt them (Pisano and Lubell 2017; 29
Geiger et al. 2019), which may depend on income and knowledge. Low-income groups may lack 30
resources to invest in refurbishments and energy-efficient technology with high upfront costs (Andrews-31
Speed and Ma 2016; Chang et al. 2009; Wolske and Stern 2018). Yet, higher income groups can afford 32
more carbon-intensive lifestyles (Golley and Meng 2012; Namazkhan et al. 2019; Frederiks et al. 2015; 33
Santillán Vera and de la Vega Navarro 2019; Mi et al. 2020; Wiedenhofer et al. 2017). Knowledge of 34
the causes and consequences of climate change and of ways to reduce GHG emissions is not always 35
accurate, but lack of knowledge is not a main barrier of mitigation actions (Boudet 2019). 36
Motivation to engage in mitigation action, reflecting individuals’ reasons for actions, depends on 37
general goals that people strive for in their life (i.e., values). People who strongly value protecting the 38
environment and other people are more likely to consider climate impacts and to engage in a wide range 39
of mitigation actions than those who strongly value individual consequences of actions, such as pleasure 40
and money (Taylor et al. 2014; Steg 2016). Values affect which types of costs and benefits people 41
consider and prioritize when making choices, including individual, affective, social, and environmental 42
costs and benefits (Gowdy 2008; Steg 2016). 43
