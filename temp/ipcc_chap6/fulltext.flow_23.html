Box 6.3 Energy Subsidies 2
Energy subsidies continue to be widely applied. Global fossil fuel subsidies represent more than half of 3
total energy subsidies with predominantly adverse environmental, economic, and social effects (high 4
confidence). 5
Energy subsidies can be defined as policy measures in the energy sector to lower the prices for 6
consumers, raise the prices for producers, or reduce energy production costs (IEA 1999). There are 7
subsidies for fossil fuels, renewables, and energy efficiency measures. The majority of the renewable 8
subsidies are generation-based incentives for solar, wind or biomass in the form of feed-in-tariffs (FIT) 9
(Chapter 13), with total annual renewable subsidy estimates of about USD 150 billion yr
-1
globally 10
(IEA 2018b). Estimates of fossil fuel subsidies can vary by an order of magnitude. For the year 2017, 11
the IEA estimated fossil fuel subsidies of USD 300 billion using IEA’s pre-tax, price-gap method (IEA 12
2018b), while the IMF included unpriced externalities in calculating subsidies of USD 5.2 trillion or 13
6.5% of global GDP (World Bank 2019; Coady et al. 2017, 2019). It has been estimated that the amount 14
spent on fossil fuel subsidies was around double the amount of subsidies spent on renewables (IEA 15
2018b). There are adverse environmental, economic and social consequences of fossil fuel subsidies 16
(Rentschler and Bazilian 2017). More than 75% of the distortions created by fossil fuel subsidies are 17
domestic, and studies indicate that reforming them can have substantial in-country benefits (Coady et 18
al. 2019, 2017). Some of the G-20 countries have implemented subsidy reforms based on low oil prices 19
(Jewell et al. 2018). 20
Fossil fuel subsidies most commonly pursue non-climate objectives, for example, enhanced access to 21
energy sources (high confidence). In some cases, these energy access subsidies have helped extend 22
modern energy sources to the poor (Kimemia and Annegarn 2016) and thereby contribute to SDG-7. 23
However, the subsidies have proven to be regressive in most cases, with little benefit reaching the poor 24
(Lockwood 2015). For example, Indonesia has introduced liquefied petroleum gas (LPG) subsidies for 25
cooking. The kerosene to LPG conversion program (“Zero Kero”) was launched in 2007 and provided 26
mainly households with free initial LPG equipment and LPG at a low subsidized price (Thoday et al. 27
2018; Imelda et al. 2018b). Besides the national government, provincial governments and industry 28
played a crucial role in implementation. Overall, the LPG conversion program in Indonesia reduced 29
cooking kerosene use (Andadari et al. 2014; Imelda et al. 2018b) and GHG emissions (Permadi et al. 30
2017) with positive health effects (Thoday et al. 2018; Imelda et al. 2018b). However, the program is 31
generally viewed as regressive and has failed to reduce traditional solid fuel use (Andadari et al. 2014; 32
Toft 2016; Thoday et al. 2018). Furthermore, even if the program decreased greenhouse gas emissions 33
relative to continued kerosene use, these subsidies are still targeted at fossil fuels and contribute to GHG 34
emissions. 35
India started a large LPG program in 2015 that provided a capital cost subsidy to poor households (e.g. 36
Kar et al. 2019; Jose et al. 2018; Gould 2018). While the program has increased adoption of LPG in 37
India (e.g. Sharma et al. 2019), it has not yet achieved a sustained use of LPG and replacement of solid 38
fuels for cooking, amplifying the need for complementary policy measures ( Gould 2018; Kar et al. 39
2019; Mani et al. 2020). The climate impacts of switching from biomass to LPG depend on the degree 40
of biomass combustion in stoves and the extent to which biomass originates from non-renewable 41
sources (Jose et al. 2018; Singh and Rao 2015). Barriers to increasing LPG use for cooking further 42
included abundance of solid fuels at zero (monetary) costs (Mani et al. 2020) as well as benefits of solid 43
fuels, such as maintaining the traditional taste of food and space heating in colder seasons (Gould 2018; 44
Sharma et al. 2020). 45
