Battery prices have dropped substantially, spurring deployment in electricity and transportation 7
Recent years have seen a rapid decline in the cost of energy storage, particularly batteries (see Section 8
6.4.4). The price of lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) has declined by 97% in the past three decades and by 9
90% in the past decade alone (IEA 2021a; Ziegler and Trancik 2021)These declines have important 10
implications for the energy systems, most notably in supporting increased deployment of variable 11
renewable electricity (VRE) generation and electrification of the vehicle fleet. 12
Battery electricity storage has emerged as an important for supporting the flexibility of electricity 13
systems as they accommodate rising shares of VRE. Although pumped-storage hydropower systems 14
accounted for 160 GW, or over 90%, of total energy storage capacity in 2019 (IEA 2020c), battery 15
energy storage systems, led by Li-ion technology, have accounted for over 90% of new capacity 16
addition since 2015 (IRENA 2019a). In 2019, 10 GW of batteries were connected at the grid and 17
consumer level, rising from 0.6 GW in 2015 (IEA 2020c; IEA WEO 2019). 18
In California, in the U.S., legislation was passed to procure around 1.3 GW energy storage (excluding 19
pumped storage) by 2020. One of the largest utility-scale battery storage facilities (300 MW) recently 20
went online in California (Vistra Corp. 2021). Other major projects are in Florida in the US (409 MW), 21
London in the UK (320 MW), Lithuania (200 MW), Australia (150 MW), Chile (112 MW) and 22
Germany (90 MW), (Katz 2020; ARENA 2020; IRENA 2019a). 23
The drop in battery prices has also had important implications in the transportation sector. Automotive 24
LIB production rose from around 40 GWh in 2015 to 160 GWh in 2020 (32%). The stock of battery 25
electric vehicles (BEVs) grew from around 0.7 million in 2015 to 4.8 million in 2019 (IEA 2020d). The 26
number of publicly accessible vehicle chargers reached 1.3 million in 2020, 30% of which were fast 27
chargers. The average battery size of BEVs reached 67 kWh in 2019 due to consumer preferences and 28
government incentives for long-range vehicles (Agency 2020; IEA 2021b). 29
The energy policy landscape continues to evolve 30
The current energy sector policy landscape consists of policy mixes or policy packages, including 31
regulatory, market-based and other approaches. These mixes have evolved over time and include many 32
sectoral but also some economy-wide policy instruments, such as carbon pricing, subsidies. 33
Governments have chosen a mix of policies and institutional mechanisms that consists of regulatory 34
instruments, like efficiency and technology standards, economic instruments (e.g. carbon pricing, 35
subsidies) (Bertram et al. 2015; Martin and Saikawa 2017) and other policies, such as government 36
interventions to provide infrastructure, information policies, and voluntary actions by non-government 37
actors (Somanathan et al. 2014). In recent years, regulatory instruments to promote low-carbon 38
infrastructure have gained traction in developing countries (Finon 2019). The choice of policies has 39
depended on institutional capacities, technological maturity and other developmental priorities of 40
governments. For example, governments have favoured regulatory instruments over economic 41
instruments when there has been sufficient institutional capacity to implement and monitor the 42
regulations and standards (Hughes and Urpelainen 2015). Furthermore, institutional capacity has also 43
determined the extent of implemented measures (Adenle et al. 2017). Market conditions and 44
