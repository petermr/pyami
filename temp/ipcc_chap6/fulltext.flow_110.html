Reducing Energy Demand. Energy service demand is expected to continue to increase with growth of 6
the economy, but there is great uncertainty about how much it will increase (Riahi et al. 2017; Bauer et 7
al. 2017; Yu et al. 2018). Given the need to produce low-carbon energy, the scale of energy demand is 8
a critical determinant of the mitigation challenge (Riahi et al. 2012). Higher energy demand calls for 9
more low-carbon energy and increases the challenge; lower energy demand reduces the need for low-10
carbon sources and therefore can ease a low-carbon transtion. Recent studies have shown that tempering 11
the growth of energy demand, while ensuring services and needs are still satisfied, can materially affect 12
the need for technological CDR (see below) (Grubler et al. 2018; van Vuuren et al. 2018). Two of the 13
Illustrative Mitigation Pathways (IMP-SP, IMP-LD) feature substantially lower final energy demand 14
across buildings, transport, and industry than most other pathways in the literature. In some cases, 15
energy demand levels are lower in 2050 (and later) than in 2019. These lower demands result in less 16
reliance on bioenergy and a more limited role for CDR. [Ch. 3, Figure 3.18] 17
6.7.1.3 Technology options to offset residual emissions 18
CDR technologies can offset emissions from sectors that are difficult to decarbonize (Section 6.6), 19
altering the timeline and character of energy sector transitions. A number of studies suggest that CDR 20

2015a; van Vuuren et al. 2018; Detz et al. 2018; Strefler et al. 2018). The reliance on CDR varies across 22
scenarios and is tightly linked future energy demand and the rate of emission reductions in the next two 23
decades: deeper near-term emissions reductions will reduce the need to rely on CDR to constrain 24
