Scaling up bioenergy use will require advanced technologies such as gasification, Fischer-Tropsch 7
processing, hydrothermal liquefaction (HTL), and pyrolysis. These pathways could deliver several final 8
energy carriers starting from multiple feedstocks, including forest biomass, dedicated cellulosic 9
feedstocks, crop residues, and wastes (Figure 6.14). While potentially cost-competitive in the future, 10
pyrolysis, Fischer-Tropsch, and HTL are not currently cost-competitive (IEA 2018c; Molino et al. 2018; 11
Prussi et al. 2019), and scaling-up these processes will require robust business strategies and optimized 12
use of co-products (Lee and Lavoie 2013). Advanced biofuels production processes are at the pilot or 13
demonstration stage and will require substantial breakthroughs or market changes to become 14
competitive. Moreover, fuels produced from these processes require upgrading to reach “drop-in” 15
conditions – that is, conditions in which they may be used directly consistent with current standards in 16
existing technologies (van Dyk et al. 2019). Additional opportunities exist to co-optimize second 17
generation biofuels and engines (Ostadi et al. 2019; Salman et al. 2020). In addition, gaseous wastes, 18
or high-moisture biomass, such as dairy manure, wastewater sludge and organic MSW could be utilized 19

A major scale-up of bioenergy production will require dedicated production of advanced biofuels. First 27
generation biofuels produced directly from food crops or animal fats both have limited potential and 28
lower yield per land area than advanced biofuels. Wastes and residues (e.g., from agricultural, forestry, 29
animal manure processing) or biomass grown on degraded, surplus, and marginal land can provide 30
opportunities for cost-effective and sustainable bioenergy at significant but limited scale (Saha and 31
