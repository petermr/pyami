6.5.2.5
Thermal power plants 6
The operation of thermal power plants will be affected by climate change, deriving from changes in the 7
ambient conditions like temperature, humidity and water availability (Schaeffer et al. 2012) (high 8
confidence). Changes in ambient temperature have relatively small impacts on coal-fired and nuclear 9
power plants (Rankine cycle); however, gas-fired power plants (Brayton or combined-cycle) may have 10
their thermal efficiency and power output significantly decreased (De Sa and Al Zubaidy 2011; 11
Schaeffer et al. 2012). Droughts decrease potential cooling water for thermal power plants and increase 12
the probability of water outlet temperatures exceeding regulatory limits, leading to lower production or 13
even shutdowns. Thermal power utilization has been reported to be on average 3.8% lower during 14
drought years globally (van Vliet et al. 2016c). and further significant decreases in available thermal 15
power plant capacity due to climate change are projected (Koch et al. 2014; van Vliet et al. 2016b; 16
Yalew et al. 2020) . An increase in climate-related nuclear power disruptions has been reported in the 17
past decades globally (Ahmad 2021). 18
Carbon capture may increase cooling water usage significantly, especially in retrofits, with up to 50% 19
increase in water usage for coal-fired power plants globally, depending on the CCS technology (Rosa 20
et al. 2020, Section 6.4). In Asia, planned coal capacity is expected to be vulnerable to droughts, sea 21
level rise, and rising air temperatures, and this may be exacerbated by incorporating carbon capture 22
(Wang et al. 2019c). Recently, however, studies have proposed designs of CCS with a minimal increase 23
in water requirements (Mikunda et al. 2021; Magneschi et al. 2017). 24
Older thermal power plants can be retrofitted to mitigate climate impacts by altering and redesigning 25
the cooling systems (Westlén 2018), although the costs for these solutions may be high. For example, 26
dry cooling may be used instead of once-through cooling; however, it lowers thermal efficiency and 27
would leave plants vulnerable to ambient temperature increase (Ahmad 2021). Closed-circuit cooling 28
is much less sensitive to water temperature than once-through cooling (Bonjean Stanton et al. 2016). 29
Modifying policies and regulation of water and heat emissions from power plants may also be used to 30
mitigate plant reliability problems induced by climate change (Eisenack 2016; Mu et al. 2020), albeit 31
with potential impacts for other water users and ecology. Improvements in water use and thermal 32
efficiencies and the use of transmission capabilities over large geographical regions to mitigate risks on 33
individual plants are also possible mitigation options (Miara et al. 2017). 34

Impacts on Energy Consumption
35
Heating demand will decrease, and cooling demand will increase in response to climate change. Peak 36
load may increase more than energy consumption, and the changing spatial and temporal load patterns 37
can impact transmission and needs for storage, demands-side management, and peak-generating 38
capacity. (high confidence) 39
