In the absence of a global comprehensive carbon price, regional regulatory policies for fossil fuels 27
supply and key demand sectors like transport, industry and buildings (Chapters 9-11), coupled with 28
regional carbon pricing instruments, were implemented to help initiating the climate actions consistent 29
with the Paris agreement (Kriegler et al. 2018). However, differences in the stringency of climate 30
regulation has triggered fear that regulation reduces the competitiveness of industries in regulated 31
countries and lead to industry re-location and “carbon leakage” (Schenker et al. 2018). In recent years, 32
however, there is little evidence of carbon leakage (Schäfer 2019; Naegele and Zaklan 2019), and even 33
positive effects of carbon pricing on efficiency have been observed (e.g., Löschel et al. 2019, for 34
German manufacturing firms, and (Germeshausen 2020) for German power plants). However, with 35
asymmetric rising carbon prices, discussions about specific policy mechanisms to address carbon 36
leakage like carbon border adjustments (Cosbey et al. 2019) were amplified. Furthermore, multiple 37
policies - often implemented by different governmental levels (national vs subnational) - interacted with 38
each other and thereby affected their environmental and economic effectiveness. Recent examples 39
include interactions of ETS with renewable support policies (e.g. Boehringer and Behrens 2015; Del 40
Rio 2017), energy efficiency policies (e.g. Wiese et al. 2018) or electricity market reform (e.g. Teng et 41
al. 2017), respectively. 42
