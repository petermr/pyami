Many alternative PV materials are improving in efficiency and stability, providing longer-term 12
pathways for continued PV costs reductions and better performance (high confidence). While solar PV 13
based on semi-conductors constructed from wafers of silicon still captures 90% of the market, new 14
designs and materials have the potential to reduce costs further, increase efficiency, reduce resource 15
use, and open new applications. The most significant technological advance within silicon PV in the 16
past ten years has been the widespread adoption of the passivated emitter and rear cell (PERC) design 17
(Green 2015), which now accounts for the majority of production. This advance boosts efficiency over 18
traditional aluminium backing by increasing reflectivity within the cell and reducing electron hole 19
recombination (Blakers 2019). Bifacial modules increase efficiency by using reflected light from the 20
ground or roof on the backside of modules (Guerrero-Lemus et al. 2016). Integrating PV into buildings 21
can reduce overall costs and improve building energy performance (Shukla et al. 2016). Concentrating 22
PV uses lenses or mirrors that collect and concentrate light onto high efficiency PV cells (Li et al. 23
2020a). Beyond crystalline silicon, thin films of amorphous silicon, cadmium telluride, and copper 24
indium gallium selenide (among others) have the potential for much lower costs while their efficiencies 25
have increased (Green et al. 2019). Perovskites, inexpensive and easy to produce crystalline structures, 26
have increased in efficiency by a factor of six in the past decade; the biggest challenge is light-induced 27
degradation as well as finding lead-free efficient compounds or establish lead recycling at the end of 28
the life cycle of the device (Petrus et al. 2017; Chang et al. 2018; Wang et al. 2019b; Zhu et al. 2020). 29
Organic solar cells are made of carbon-based semiconductors like the ones found in the displays made 30
from organic light emitting diodes (OLEDs) and can be processed in thin films on large areas with 31
scalable and fast coating processes on plastic substrates. The main challenges are raising the efficiency 32
and improving their lifetime (Ma et al. 2020; Riede et al. 2021). Quantum dots, spherical semi-33
conductor nano-crystals, can be tuned to absorb specific wavelengths of sunlight giving them the 34
potential for high efficiency with very little material use (Kramer et al. 2015). A common challenge for 35
all emerging solar cell technologies is developing the corresponding production equipment. Hybrids of 36
silicon with layers of quantum dots and perovskites have the potential to take advantage of the benefits 37
of all three, although those designs require that these new technologies have stability and scale that 38
match those of silicon (Palmstrom et al. 2019; Chang et al. 2017). This broad array of alternatives to 39
making PV from crystalline silicon offer realistic potential for lower costs, reduced material use, and 40
higher efficiencies in future years (Victoria et al. 2021). 41
Besides PV, alternative solar technologies exist, including CSP, which can provide special services in 42
high-temperature heat and diurnal storage, even if it is more costly than PV and its potential for 43
deployment is limited. CSP uses reflective surfaces, such as parabolic mirrors, to focus sunlight on a 44
receiver to heat a working fluid, which is subsequently transformed into electricity (Islam et al. 2018). 45
Solar heating and cooling are also well established technologies, and solar energy can be utilized 46

al. 2018). Solar chimneys, still purely conceptual, heat air using large transparent greenhouse-like 48
