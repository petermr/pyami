6.4.3.1
Role of flexibility technologies 9
Flexibility technologies - including energy storage, demand-side response, flexible/dispatchable 10
generation, grid forming converters, and transmission interconnection - as well as advanced control 11
systems, can facilitate cost-effective and secure low-carbon energy systems (high confidence). 12
Flexibility technologies have already been implemented, but they can be enhanced and deployed more 13
widely. Due to their interdependencies and similarities, there can be both synergies and conflicts for 14
utilizing these flexibility options (Bistline et al. 2021). It will therefore be important to coordinate the 15
deployment of the potential flexibility technologies and smart control strategies. Important electricity 16
system flexibility options include the following: 17
•
Flexible/dispatchable generation: Advances in generation technologies, for example, gas/hydrogen 18
plants and nuclear plants, can enable them to provide flexibility services. These technologies would 19
start more quickly, operate at lower power output, and make faster output changes, enabling more 20
secure and cost-effective integration of VRE generation and end-use electrification. There are 21
already important developments in increasing nuclear plants flexibility (e.g., in France (Office of 22
Nuclear Energy 2021)) and the development of small modular reactors, which could support system 23
balancing (FTI Consulting 2018). 24
•
Grid-forming converters (inverters): The transition from conventional electricity generation, 25
applying mainly synchronous machines to inverter-dominated renewable generation, creates 26
significant operating challenges. These challenges are mainly associated with reduced synchronous 27
inertia, system stability, and black start capability. Grid-forming converters will be a cornerstone 28
for the control of future electricity systems dominated by VRE generation. These converters will 29
address critical stability challenges, including the lack of system inertia, frequency and voltage 30
regulation, and black-start services while reducing or eliminating the need to operate conventional 31
generation (Tayyebi et al. 2019). 32
•
Interconnection: Electricity interconnections between different regions can facilitate more cost-33
effective renewable electricity deployment. Interconnection can enable large-scale sharing of 34
energy and provide balancing services. Back-up energy carriers beyond electricity, such as 35
ammonia, can be shared through gas/ammonia/hydrogen-based interconnections, strengthening 36
temporal coupling of multiple sectors in different regions (Bhagwat et al. 2017; Brown et al. 2018) 37
(Section 6.4.5). 38
•
Demand-side response: Demand-side schemes – including, for example, smart appliances, EVs, 39
and building-based thermal energy storage (Heleno et al. 2014) – can provide flexibility services 40
across multiple time frames and systems. Through differentiation between essential and non-41
essential needs during emergency conditions, smart control of demands can significantly enhance 42
system resilience (Chaffey 2016). 43
•
Energy storage: Energy storage technologies (Section 6.4.4) can act as both demand and generation 44
sources. They can provide services such as system balancing, various ancillary services, and 45
network management. Long-duration energy storage can significantly enhance the utilization of 46
renewable energy sources and reduce the need for firm low-carbon generation (Sepulveda et al. 47
2021). 48
