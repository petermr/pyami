Drawbacks of batteries include relatively short lifespans and the use of hazardous or costly materials in 2
some variants. While LIB costs are decreasing (Schmidt et al. 2017; Vartiainen et al. 2020), the risk of 3
thermal runaway, which could ignite a fire (Gur 2018; Wang et al. 2019a), concerns about long-term 4
resource availability (Sun et al. 2017; Olivetti et al. 2017), and concerns about global cradle-to-grave 5
impacts (Peters et al. 2017; Kallitsis et al. 2020) need to be addressed. 6
The superior characteristics of LIBs will keep them the dominant choice for EV and grid applications 7
in the medium-term (high confidence). There are, however, several next-generation battery chemistries 8
(Placke et al. 2017), which show promise (high confidence). Cost reductions through economies of scale 9
are a key area for development. Extending the life of the battery can bring down overall costs and 10
mitigate the environmental impacts (Peters et al. 2017). Understanding and controlling battery 11
degradation is therefore important. The liquid, air-reactive electrolytes of conventional LIBs are the 12
main source of their safety issues (Gur 2018; Janek and Zeier 2016), so All-Solid-State Batteries, in 13
which the electrolyte is a solid, stable material, are being developed. They are expected to be safe, be 14
durable, and have higher energy densities (Janek and Zeier 2016). New chemistries and concepts are 15
being explored, such as lithium-sulfur batteries to achieve even higher energy densities (Van Noorden 16
2014; Blomgren 2017) and sodium chemistries because sodium is more abundant than lithium (Hwang 17
et al. 2017). Cost-effective recycling of batteries will address many sustainability issues and prevent 18
hazardous and wasteful disposal of used batteries (Harper et al. 2019). Post-LIB chemistries include 19
metal sulfur, metal-air, metal ion (besides Li) and All-Solid-State Batteries. 20
Compressed Air Energy Storage (CAES). With CAES, off-peak electricity is used to compress air in a 21
reservoir – either in salt caverns for large scale or in high-pressure tanks for smaller-scale installations. 22
The air is later released to generate electricity. While conventional CAES has used natural gas to power 23
compression, new low carbon CAES technologies, such as isothermal or adiabatic CAES, control 24
thermal losses during compression and expansion (Wang et al. 2017c). Fast responses and higher 25
efficiencies occur in small-scale CAES installations, scalable to suit the application as a distributed 26
energy store, offering a flexible, low maintenance alternative (Luo et al. 2014; Venkataramani et al. 27
2016). 28
CAES is a mature technology in use since the 1970s. Although CAES technologies have been 29
developed, there are not many installations at present (Blanc et al. 2020; Wang et al. 2017b). While the 30
opportunities for CAES are significant, with a global geological storage potential of about 6.5 PW 31
(Aghahosseini and Breyer 2018), a significant amount of initial investment is required. Higher 32
efficiencies and energy densities can be achieved by exploiting the hydrostatic pressure of deep water 33
to compress air within submersible reservoirs (Pimm et al. 2014). CAES is best suited to bulk diurnal 34
electricity storage for buffering VRE sources and services, which do not need a very rapid response. In 35
contrast to PHS, CAES has far more siting options and poses few environmental impacts. 36
Liquid Air Energy Storage (LAES). Liquid air energy storage uses electricity to liquefy air by cooling 37
it to –196°C and storing it in this condensed form (largely liquid nitrogen) in large, insulated tanks. To 38
release electricity, the ‘liquid air’ is evaporated through heating, expanding to drive gas turbines. Low-39
grade waste heat can be utilized, providing opportunities for integrating with industrial processes to 40
increase system efficiency. There are clear, exploitable synergies with the existing liquid gas 41
infrastructure (Peters and Sievert 2016). 42
