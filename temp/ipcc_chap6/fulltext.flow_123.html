6.7.6.1
Strategies to encourage climate mitigation actions 1
Climate policy will be particularly effective if it targets key factor inhibiting, enabling, and motivating 2
mitigation behaviours. As barriers differ across mitigation options, regions, and groups, tailored 3
approaches are more effective (Grubb et al. 2017). When people face important barriers to change (e.g., 4
high costs, legal barriers), policy would be needed make low carbon actions more attractive, or to make 5
high carbon actions less attractive. As people generally face multiple barriers for change, combinations 6
of policies would be more effective (Rosenow et al. 2017). 7
Financial incentives can motivate mitigation actions (Santos 2008; Thøgersen 2009; Eliasson 2014; 8
Maki et al. 2016; Bolderdijk et al. 2011), particularly when actions are costly (Mundaca 2007). In many 9
countries, more residential solar PV were installed after the introduction of favourable financial 10
schemes such as feed-in-tariffs, federal income tax credits, and net metering (Wolske and Stern 2018). 11
Similarly, a subsidy promoted the installation of solar water heaters in Asia (Chang et al. 2009). Yet, 12
financial incentives may underperform expectations when other factors are overlooked. For example, 13
people may not respond to financial incentives when they do not trust the organization sponsoring the 14
program or when it takes too much effort to receive the incentive (Mundaca 2007; Stern et al. 2016a). 15
Financial incentives are more effective if combined with strategies addressing non-financial barriers. 16
Communicating financial consequences of behaviour seems less effective than emphasizing social 17
rewards (Handgraaf et al. 2013) or benefits of actions for people (e.g., public health, comfort) and the 18
environment (Asensio and Delmas 2015, 2016; Schwartz et al. 2015; Ossokina 2020; Bolderdijk et al. 19
2013). Financial appeals may have limited effects because they reduce people’s focus on environmental 20
consequences, weaken intrinsic motivation to engage in mitigation actions, provide a license to pollute 21
(Agrawal et al. 2015; Bolderdijk and Steg 2015; Schwartz et al. 2015), and because pursuing small 22
financial gains is perceived not worth the effort (Dogan et al. 2014; Bolderdijk et al. 2013). 23
Providing information on the causes and consequences of climate change or on effective mitigation 24
actions increases people’s knowledge and awareness, but generally does not promote mitigation actions 25
by individuals (Abrahamse et al. 2005) or organizations (Anderson and Newell 2004). Fear-inducing 26
representations of climate change may inhibit action when they make people feel helpless (O´Neill and 27
Nicholson-Cole 2009). Energy-related advice and feedback can promote energy savings, load shifting 28
in electricity use and sustainable travel, particularly when framed in terms of losses rather than gains 29
(Gonzales et al. 1988; Wolak 2011; Bradley et al. 2016; Bager and Mundaca 2017). Also, credible and 30
targeted information at the point of decision can promote action (Stern et al. 2016a). Information is 31
more effective when delivered by a trusted source, such as peers (Palm 2017), advocacy groups (Schelly 32
2014), and community organizations (Noll et al. 2014), and when tailored to actors’ personal situation 33
and core values (Abrahamse et al. 2007; Boomsma and Steg 2014; van den Broek et al. 2017; Daamen 34
et al. 2001; Wolsko et al. 2016; Bolderdijk et al. 2013). This explains why home energy audits promoted 35
energy savings (Delmas et al. 2013; Alberini and Towe 2015), and investments in resource efficiency 36
and renewable energy generation (Kastner and Stern 2015). 37
Energy use feedback can promote energy saving behaviour within households (Grønhøj and Thøgersen 38
2011; Fischer 2008; Karlin et al. 2015; Delmas et al. 2013; Zangheri et al. 2019) and at work (Young 39
et al. 2015), particularly when provided in real-time or immediately after the action so that people learn 40
the impact of different actions (Faruqui et al. 2009; Delmas et al. 2013; Abrahamse et al. 2005; 41
Tiefenbeck et al. 2016; Stern et al. 2016a; Yu et al. 2015). Energy labels (Banerjee and Solomon 2003; 42
Stadelmann 2017), visualization techniques (Pahl et al. 2016), and ambient persuasive technology 43
(Midden and Ham 2012) can encourage energy savings as they immediately make sense and hardly 44
require users’ conscious attention. Feedback can make people aware of their previous mitigation 45
behaviours, which can strengthen their environmental self-identity, and motivate them to engage in 46
other mitigation actions as well as to act in line with their self-image (Van der Werff et al. 2014). 47
