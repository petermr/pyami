Social influence approaches that communicate what other people do or think can encourage mitigation 1
actions (Clayton et al. 2015), as can social models of desired actions (Osbaldiston and Schott 2012; 2
Abrahamse and Steg 2013; Wolske et al. 2020; Sussman and Gifford 2013). Feedback on one’s own 3
energy use relative to others can be effective (Nolan et al. 2008; Allcott 2011; Schultz et al. 2015), 4
although not always, and effect sizes are small (Abrahamse and Steg 2013) compared to other types of 5
feedback (Karlin et al. 2015). 6
Interventions that capitalize on people’s motivation to be consistent can promote mitigation actions 7
(Steg 2016). Examples are commitment strategies where people pledge to act (Abrahamse and Steg 8
2013; Lokhorst et al. 2013), implementation intentions where they additionally explicate how and when 9
they will perform the relevant action and how they would cope with possible barriers (Rees et al. 2018; 10
Bamberg 2000, 2002), and hypocrisy-related strategies that make people aware of inconsistencies 11
between their attitudes and behaviour (Osbaldiston and Schott 2012). 12
Bottom-up approaches can promote mitigation action (Abrahamse and Steg 2013). Indeed, community 13
energy initiatives can encourage members’ low carbon behaviour (Middlemiss 2011; Seyfang and 14
Haxeltine 2012; Abrahamse and Steg 2013; Sloot et al. 2018). Organizations can promote mitigation 15
behaviour among their employees and customers by communicating their mission and strategies to 16
mitigate climate change (van der Werff et al. 2021; Ruepert et al. 2017). 17
Default options, where a preset choice is implemented if users do not select another option, can promote 18
mitigation actions such as energy savings, green electricity uptake, and meat-free meals options (Liebe 19
et al. 2021; Pichert and Katsikopoulos 2008; Ölander and Thøgersen 2014; Kunreuther and Weber 20
2014; Bessette et al. 2014; Ebeling and Lotz 2015; Liebe et al. 2018; Campbell-Arvai et al. 2014). 21
6.7.6.2
Acceptability of policy, mitigation options and system changes 22
Public acceptability reflects the extent to which the public evaluates climate policy, mitigation options, 23
and system changes (un)favourably, which can shape, enable, or prevent low-carbon energy system 24
transitions. Public acceptability of policy and mitigation options is higher when people expect these 25
have more positive and less negative consequences for self, others, and the environment (Demski et al. 26
2015; Drews and Van den Bergh 2016; Perlaviciute and Steg 2014). Public opposition may result when 27
a culturally valued landscape is affected by renewable energy development (Warren et al. 2005; Devine-28
Wright and Howes 2010), particularly place-based identities are threatened (Devine-Wright 2009, 2013; 29
Boudet 2019). Acceptability can increase after a policy or change has been implemented and the 30
consequences appear to be more positive than expected (Carattini et al. 2018; Schuitema et al. 2010; 31
Eliasson 2014; Weber 2015); effective policy trials can thus build public support. 32
Next, climate policy and low carbon options are evaluated as more fair and acceptable when costs and 33
benefits are distributed equally, and when nature, the environment and future generations are protected 34
(Schuitema et al. 2011; Drews and Van den Bergh 2016). Compensating affected groups for losses due 35
to policy or systems changes enhanced public acceptability in some cases (Perlaviciute and Steg 2014), 36
but people may disagree on which compensation would be worthwhile (Aitken 2010b; Cass et al. 2010), 37
on the distribution of compensation (Devine-Wright and Sherry-Brennan 2019; Leer Jørgensen et al. 38
2020), or feel they are being bribed (Perlaviciute and Steg 2014; Cass et al. 2010). Pricing policies are 39
more acceptable when revenues are earmarked for environmental purposes (Steg et al. 2006; Sælen and 40
Kallbekken 2011) or redistributed towards those affected (Schuitema and Steg 2008). 41
Climate policy and mitigation options, such as renewable energy projects, are also perceived as more 42
fair and acceptable when the public (Dietz 2013; Bidwell 2014; Bernauer et al. 2016b) or public society 43
organizations (Terwel et al. 2010; Bernauer et al. 2016b) could participate in the decision making 44
(Devine-Wright 2005; Terwel et al. 2012; Perlaviciute and Squintani 2020; Arvai 2003; Walker and 45
Baxter 2017). People are more motivated to participate in decision making on local projects than on 46
