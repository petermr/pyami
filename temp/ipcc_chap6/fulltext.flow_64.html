6.4.5.2
Electricity Transmission 13
Given the significant geographical variations in the efficiency of renewable resources across different 14
regions and continents, electricity transmission could facilitate cost effective deployment of renewable 15
generation, enhance resilience and security of supply, and increase operational efficiency (high 16
confidence). The diurnal and seasonal characteristics of different renewable energy sources such as 17
wind, solar, and hydropower can vary significantly by location. Through enhanced electricity 18
transmission infrastructure, more wind turbines can be deployed in areas with high wind potential and 19
more solar panels in areas with larger solar irradiation. Increases in electricity transmission and trade 20
can also enhance operational efficiency and reduce or defer the need for investment in peaking plants, 21
storage, or other load management techniques needed to meet security of supply requirements 22
associated with localized use of VRE sources. Increased interconnectivity of large-scale grids also 23
allows the aggregation of ‘smart grid’ solutions such as flexible heating and cooling devices for flexible 24
demand in industrial, commercial, and domestic sectors (Hakimi et al. 2020) and EVs (Li et al. 2021; 25
Muratori and Mai 2020). In general, interconnection is more cost-optimal for countries that are 26
geographically close to each other and can benefit from the diversity of their energy mixes and usage 27
(Schlachtberger et al. 2017). Such developments are not without price, however, and amongst other 28
concerns, raise issues surrounding land use, public acceptance, and resource acquisition for materials 29
necessary for renewable developments (Vakulchuk et al. 2020; Capellán-Pérez et al. 2017). 30
A number of studies have demonstrated the cost benefits of interconnected grids in a range of 31
geographical settings, including across the United States (Bloom et al. 2020), across Europe (2020; 32
Newbery et al. 2013; Cluet et al. 2020), between Australia and parts of Asia (Halawa et al. 2018), and 33
broader global regions, for example between the Middle East and Europe or North Africa and Europe 34
(Tsoutsos et al. 2015). While there is growing interest in interconnection among different regions or 35
continents, a broad range of geopolitical and socio-techno-economic challenges would need to be 36
overcome to support this level of international co-operation and large-scale network expansion (Bertsch 37
et al. 2017; Palle 2021). 38
