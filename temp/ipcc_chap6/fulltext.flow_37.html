Despite low probabilities, the potential for major nuclear accidents exists, and the radiation exposure 25
impacts could be large and long-lasting (Steinhauser et al. 2014). However, new reactor designs with 26
passive and enhanced safety systems reduce the risk of such accidents significantly (high confidence). 27
The (normal) activity of a nuclear reactor results in low volumes of radioactive waste, which requires 28
strictly controlled and regulated disposal. On a global scale, roughly 421 ktons of spent nuclear fuel 29
have been produced since 1971 (IEA 2014). Out of this volume, 2–3% is high-level radioactive waste, 30
which presents challenges in terms of radiotoxicity and decay longevity, and ultimately entails 31
permanent disposal. 32
Nuclear energy is found to be favourable regarding land occupation (Cheng and Hammond 2017; 33
Luderer et al. 2019) and ecological impacts (Brook and Bradshaw 2015; Gibon et al. 2017). Similarly, 34
bulk material requirements per unit of energy produced are low (e.g. aluminum, copper, iron, rare earth 35
metals) (Vidal et al. 2013; Luderer et al. 2019). Water-intensive inland nuclear power plants may 36
contribute to localized water stress and competition for water uses. The choice of cooling systems 37
(closed-loop instead of once-through) can significantly moderate withdrawal rates of the freshwater (Jin 38
et al. 2019; Fricko et al. 2016; Mouratiadou et al. 2016; Meldrum et al. 2013). Reactors situated on the 39
seashore are not affected by water scarcity issues (JRC EU 2021). Life cycle assessment (LCA) studies 40
suggest that the overall impacts on human health (in terms of disability adjusted life years (DALYs) 41
from the normal operation of nuclear power plants are substantially lower than those caused by fossil 42
fuel technologies and are comparable to renewable energy sources (Treyer et al. 2014; Gibon et al. 43
2017). 44
