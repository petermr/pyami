Wind farms can cause local ecological impacts, including impacts on animal habitat and movements, 3
biological concerns, bird and bat fatalities from collisions with rotating blades, and health concerns 4
(Morrison and Sinclair 2004). The impacts on animal habitats and collisions can be resolved or reduced 5
by selectively stopping some wind turbines in high risk locations, often without affecting the 6
productivity of the wind farm (de Lucas et al. 2012). Many countries now require environmental studies 7
of impacts of wind turbines on wildlife prior to project development, and, in some regions, shutdowns 8
are required during active bird migration (de Lucas et al. 2012). Offshore wind farms can also impact 9
migratory birds and other sea species (Hooper et al. 2017). Floating foundations pose lower 10
environmental impacts at build stage (IRENA 2019c), but their cumulative long-term impacts are 11
unclear (Goodale and Milman 2016). Recent studies find weak associations between wind farm noise 12
and measures of long-term human health (Poulsen et al. 2018a,b, 2019a,b).

Public support for onshore and particularly offshore wind energy is generally high, although people 14
may oppose specific wind farm projects (high confidence) (e.g., Rand and Hoen 2017; Steg 2018; Bell 15
et al. 2005; Batel and Devine-Wright 2015). People generally believe that wind energy is associated 16
with environmental benefits and that it is relatively cheap. Yet, some people believe wind turbines can 17
cause noise and visual aesthetic pollution, threaten places of symbolic value (Russell et al. 2020; 18
Devine-Wright and Wiersma 2020), and have adverse effects on wildlife (Bates and Firestone 2015), 19
which challenges public acceptability (Rand and Hoen 2017). Support for local wind projects is higher 20
when people believe fair decision-making procedures have been implemented (Aitken 2010a; Dietz and 21
Stern 2008). Evidence is mixed whether distance from wind turbines or financial compensation 22
increases public acceptability of wind turbines (Hoen et al. 2019; Rand and Hoen 2017; Cass et al. 23
2010; Rudolph et al. 2018). Offshore wind farms projects have higher public support, but can also face 24
resistance (Rudolph et al. 2018; Bidwell 2017). 25
Common economic barriers to wind development are high initial cost of capital, long payback periods, 26
and inadequate access to capital. Optimal wind energy expansion is most likely to occur in the presence 27
of a political commitment to establish, maintain, and improve financial support instruments, 28
technological efforts to support a local supply chains, and grid investments integrate VRE electricity 29
(Diógenes et al. 2020). 30

Box 6.4 Critical strategic minerals and a low-carbon energy system transition 32
The secure supply of many metals and minerals (e.g., cobalt, copper, lithium, and rare earth elements, 33
REEs) is critical to supporting a low-emissions energy system transition (Sovacool et al. 2020). A low-34
carbon energy system transition will increase the demand for these minerals to be used in technologies 35
like wind turbines, PV cells, and batteries (World Bank 2020). Reliance on these minerals has raised 36
questions about possible constraints to a low-carbon energy system transition, including supply chain 37
disruptions (Chapter 10.6). Concerns have also been raised about mining for these materials, which 38
frequently results in severe environmental impacts (Sonter et al. 2020), and metal production itself is 39
energy-intensive and difficult to decarbonize (Sovacool et al. 2020). 40
Wind energy depends on two critical REEs - neodymium and dysprosium - used in magnets in high-41
performance generators (Pavel et al. 2017; Li et al. 2020b). Silicon-wafer-based solar PV, which 42
accounted for 95% of PV production in 2020, does not use REEs but utilizes aluminium, copper, and 43
silver (IEA 2021a). Lithium, nickel, cobalt, and phosphorous are used in batteries. Many critical 44
minerals are used in EVs, including aluminium and copper in manufacturing the necessary EV charging 45
infrastructure, and neodymium in permanent magnet motors. 46
