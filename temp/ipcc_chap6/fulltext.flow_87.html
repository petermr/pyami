Box 6.8 100% Renewables in Net Zero Energy Systems 7
The decreasing cost and increasing performance of renewable energy has generated interest in the 8
feasibility of providing nearly all energy services with renewables. Renewable energy includes wind 9
power, solar power, hydroelectric power, bioenergy, geothermal energy, tidal power, and ocean power. 10
There are two primary frames around which 100% renewable energy systems are discussed: 100% 11
renewable electricity systems and 100% renewable energy systems, considering not only electricity but 12
all aspects of the energy system. 13
It is technically feasible to use very high renewable shares (e.g., above 75% of annual regional 14
generation) to meet hourly electricity demand under a range of conditions, especially when VRE 15
options, notably wind and solar, are complemented by other resources (high confidence). There are 16
currently many grids with high renewable shares and large anticipated roles for VRE sources, in 17
particular wind and solar (see Section 6.4), in future low-carbon electricity systems. An increasingly 18
large set of studies examines the feasibility of high renewable penetration and economic drivers under 19
different policy, technology, and market scenarios (Denholm et al. 2021; Blanford et al. 2021; Bistline 20
et al. 2019; Hansen et al. 2019; Jenkins et al. 2018b; Cochran et al. 2014; Dowling et al. 2020; Deason 21
2018). High wind and solar penetration involves technical and economic challenges due to their unique 22
characteristics such as spatial and temporal variability, short- and long-term uncertainty, and non-23
synchronous generation (Cole et al. 2017). These challenges become increasingly important as 24
renewable shares approach 100% (Sections 6.6.2.2 and 6.4.3). 25
There are many balancing options in systems with very high renewables (Denholm et al. 2021; Bistline 26
2021a; Mai et al. 2018; Milligan et al. 2015; Jenkins et al. 2018b). 27
●
Energy storage: Energy storage technologies like batteries, pumped hydro, and hydrogen can 28
provide a range of system services (Balducci et al. 2018; Bistline et al. 2020a; Section 6.4.4). 29
Lithium-ion batteries have received attention as costs fall and installations increase, but very high 30
renewable shares typically entail either dispatchable generation or long-duration storage in addition 31
to short-duration options (Schill 2020; Arbabzadeh et al. 2019; Jenkins et al. 2018b). Energy storage 32
technologies are part of a broad set of options (including synchronous condensers, demand-side 33
measures, and even inverter-based technologies themselves) for providing grid services (Castillo 34
and Gayme 2014; EPRI 2019a). 35
●
Transmission and trade: To balance differences in resource availability, high renewable systems 36
will very likely entail investments in transmission capacity (Zappa et al. 2019; Pleßmann and 37
Blechinger 2017; Macdonald et al. 2016; Mai and Et al 2014; Section 6.4.5) and changes in trade 38
(Abrell and Rausch 2016; Bistline et al. 2019). These increases will likely be accompanied by 39
expanded balancing regions to take advantage of geographical smoothing. 40
●
Dispatchable (“on-demand”) generation: Dispatchable generation could include flexible fossil 41
units or low-carbon fuels such as hydrogen with lower minimum load levels (Bistline 2019; 42
Denholm et al. 2018), renewables like hydropower, geothermal, or biomass (Hansen et al. 2019; 43
Hirth 2016), or flexible nuclear (Jenkins et al. 2018a). The composition depends on costs and other 44
policy goals, though in all cases, capacity factors are low for these resources (Mills et al. 2020). 45
