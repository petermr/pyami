Box 6.9 The Hydrogen Economy 2
The phrase “hydrogen economy” is often used to describe future energy systems in which hydrogen 3
plays a prominent role. These future energy systems would not use hydrogen for all end uses; they 4
would use hydrogen to complement other energy carriers, mainly electricity, where hydrogen might 5
have advantages. Hydrogen could provide long-term electricity storage to support high-penetration of 6
intermittent renewables and could enable trading and storage of electricity between different regions to 7
overcome seasonal or production capability differences (Dowling et al. 2020; Sepulveda et al. 2021). It 8
could also be used in lieu of natural gas for peaking generation, provide process heat for industrial 9
needs, or be used in the metal sector via direct reduction of iron ore (Chapter 11). Clean hydrogen could 10
be used as a feedstock in the production of various chemicals and synthetic hydrocarbons. Finally, 11
hydrogen-based fuel cells could power vehicles. Recent advances in battery storage make electric 12
vehicles the most attractive alternative for light-duty transport. However, fuel cell technology could 13
complement electric vehicles in supporting the decarbonisation of heavy-duty transport segments (e.g., 14
trucks, buses, ships, and trains) (Chapter 10). 15
Hydrogen production costs have historically been prohibitive, but recent technological developments 16
are bringing costs down. These developments include improvements in hydrogen production 17
technologies in terms of efficiency and capital costs (e.g., SMR) (Alrashed and Zahid 2021; Boretti and 18
Banik 2021) and the emergence of alternative production technologies such as electrolysers (Dawood 19
et al. 2020). These technological changes, along with decreasing costs of renewable power, are 20
increasing the viability of hydrogen. Other improvements in hydrogen-based technologies are also 21
emerging quickly. Gas turbines now run on blended fuels containing 5-95% hydrogen by volume (GE 22
2020) and could operate entirely on hydrogen by 2030 (Pflug et al. 2019). Fuel cell costs have decreased 23
by 80-95% since the early 2000s, while power density and durability have improved (Kurtz et al. 2019; 24
IEA 2019e; Jouin et al. 2016). 25
For hydrogen to support decarbonisation, it will need to be produced from zero-carbon or extremely 26
low-carbon energy sources. One such production category is “green hydrogen.” While there is no 27
unified definition for green hydrogen, it can be produced by the electrolysis of water using electricity 28
generated without carbon emissions (such as renewables). Hydrogen can also be produced through 29
biomass gasification with CCS (BECCS), leading to negative carbon emissions (del Pozo et al. 2021)
