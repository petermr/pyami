Energy Security. Countries emphasizing import security will tend to rely more heavily on indigenous 11
resources (Section 6.3). Some indigenous resources may raise security of supply issues that will 12
influence energy system configurations. Bioenergy and hydropower, for example, can be subject to 13
import climate risks (6.5), and significant integration of VRE technologies will influence electricity 14
system infrastructure and management (6.6.2, Box 6.8). 15
Other Factors. Countries will consider a wide range of other factors in building toward low-carbon 16
energy systems. Population density, for example, will influence building and transportation energy 17
demands; economic transitions will influence industrial energy demands. Societal priorities beyond 18
climate, notably SDGs may influence technology choices and types of energy systems (Sections 6.3 19
and 6.7.7). 20

Box 6.10 Regional Integration of Energy Systems 22
Energy systems are linked across countries in many ways: countries transport crude oil across the ocean 23
in supertankers, pipelines carry oil and natural gas across country boundaries, electric power lines cross 24
country boundaries, and countries trade industrial commodities that carry embodied energy or that are 25
essential inputs to mitigation technologies. Future systems will generate electricity using different 26
mixes of technologies, produce and transport different carriers (e.g., hydrogen or biofuels), and use far 27
less fossil fuel, among other major changes. Important examples include electricity, hydrogen, and 28
biomass. 29
Electricity System Integration. Net-zero energy systems will rely more heavily on electricity 30
generated from low-emissions technologies. Given the significant variations in the location of low-31
carbon electricity resources and the temporal variability of some renewable electricity sources, notably 32
solar and wind power, regional electricity grids could reduce overall costs of net zero energy systems 33
(Section 6.4.5). Furthermore, electricity transmission interconnections could significantly reduce local 34
energy balancing costs and investment in peaking plants needed to meet security of supply 35
requirements, and it could increase system resilience, especially in the case of extreme events such as 36
heat waves or cold spells (Fasihi and Bogdanov 2016). Important challenges to regional electricity 37
integration include geopolitical concerns from cross-border trade and societal and technological 38
challenges associated with building new transmission lines. 39
