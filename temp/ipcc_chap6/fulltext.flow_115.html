6.7.3.1
Societal and Institutional Inertia 2
A combination of factors - user, business, cultural, regulatory, and transnational - will hinder low-3
carbon energy transitions. Strong path dependencies, even in early formative stages, can have lasting 4
impacts on energy systems, producing inertia that cuts across technological, economic, institutional and 5
political dimensions (high confidence) (Vadén et al. 2019; Rickards et al. 2014) Chapter 5). 6
Energy systems exemplify the ways in which massive volumes of labor, capital, and effort become sunk 7
into particular institutional configurations (Bridge et al. 2013, 2018). Several embedded factors affect 8
large-scale transformation of these systems and make technological diffusion a complex process: 9
●
User environments affect purchase activities and can involve the integration of new technologies 10
into user practices and the development of new preferences, routines, habits and even values 11
(Kanger et al. 2019). 12
●
Business environments can shape the development of industries, business models, supply and 13
distribution chains, instrument constituencies and repair facilities (Béland and Howlett 2016). 14
●
Culture can encompass the articulation of positive discourses, narratives, and visions that enhance 15
cultural legitimacy and societal acceptance of new technologies. Regulatory embedding can capture 16
the variety of policies that shape production, markets and use of new technologies. 17
●
Transnational community can reflect a shared understanding in a community of global experts 18
related to new technologies that transcends the borders of a single place, often a country. 19
While low-carbon innovation involves systemic change (Geels et al. 2018), these are typically less 20
popular than energy supply innovations among policymakers and the wider public. Managing low 21
carbon transitions is therefore not only a techno-managerial challenge (based on targets, policies, and 22
expert knowledge), but also a broader political project that involves the building of support coalitions 23
that include businesses and civil society (moderate evidence, high agreement). 24
