Hydrogen can be liquefied and transported at volume over the ocean without pressurization. This 5
requires a temperature of –253ºC and is therefore energy-intensive and costly (Niermann et al. 2021). 6
Once it reaches its destination, the hydrogen needs to be re-gasified, adding further cost. A 7
demonstration project is under development exporting liquid hydrogen from Australia to Japan 8
(Yamashita et al. 2019). Hydrogen could also be transported as ammonia by ocean in liquid form. 9
Ammonia is advantageous because it is easier to store than hydrogen (Zamfirescu and Dincer 2008; 10
Nam et al. 2018; Soloveichik 2016). Liquid ammonia requires temperatures below –33ºC and is 11
therefore more straightforward and less costly to transport than liquified hydrogen and even liquified 12
natural gas (Singh and Sahu 2018). A project exporting ammonia from Saudi Arabia to Japan is under 13
consideration (Nagashima 2018). LOHCs could also be used to transport hydrogen at ambient 14
temperature and pressure. This advantageous property of LOHCs makes them similar to oil products, 15
meaning they can be transported in existing oil infrastructure including oil tankers and tanks (Niermann 16
et al. 2019; IEA 2019). A project is under development to export hydrogen from Brunei to Japan using 17
LOHCs (Kurosaki 2018). 18

Intra-Regional Hydrogen Transportation. Within a country or region, hydrogen would likely be 24
pressurized and delivered as compressed gas. About three times as much compressed hydrogen by 25
volume is required to supply the same amount of energy as natural gas. Security of supply is therefore 26
more challenging in hydrogen networks than in natural gas networks. Storing hydrogen in pipelines 27
(linepack) would be important to maintaining security of supply (Ameli et al. 2019, 2017). Due to the 28
physics of hydrogen, in most cases exiting gas infrastructure would need to be upgraded to transport 29
hydrogen. Transporting hydrogen in medium- or high-pressure networks most often would require 30
