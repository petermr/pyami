Although there are no technical upper bounds on renewable electricity penetration, the economic value 13
of additional wind and solar capacity typically decreases as their penetration rises, creating economic 14

Energy systems that are 100% renewable (including all parts of the energy sector, and not only 20
electricity generation) raise a range of technological, regulatory, market, and operational challenges that 21
make their competitiveness uncertain (high confidence). These systems require decarbonizing all 22
electricity, using this zero-carbon electricity broadly, and then utilizing zero-carbon energy carriers for 23
all end uses not served by electricity, for example, air travel, long-distance transport, and high-24
temperature process heat. Broader questions emerge regarding the attractiveness of supplying all 25
energy, and not just electricity, with renewables (Figure 6.22). Integrated assessment and energy 26
systems research suggest large roles for renewables, but energy and electricity shares are far from 100%, 27
even with stringent emissions reductions targets and optimistic assumptions about future cost reductions 28
(Huntington et al. 2020; Jenkins et al. 2018b; Bauer et al. 2018; Bistline et al. 2018, Section 6.7.1). 29
Scenarios with 100% renewable energy systems are an emerging subset in the decarbonization 30
literature, especially at regional levels (Denholm et al. 2021; Hansen et al. 2019). Many 100% 31
renewables studies focus more heavily on electrification for decarbonizing end uses, and include less 32
biofuels and hydrogen than the broader literature on deep decarbonization (Bauer et al. 2018a). These 33
studies typically assume a constrained set of available technologies to demonstrate the technical 34
feasibility of very high renewable systems and do not optimize to find least-cost, technology-neutral 35
decarbonization pathways, and many 100% renewables studies focus on the electricity sector or a 36
limited number of sectors (Hansen et al. 2019; Jenkins et al. 2018a). In addition to renewables, studies 37
broadly agree that including additional low-carbon options – including not only low-carbon electricity 38
but also targeted use of fossil fuels with and without CCS (Section 6.6.2.1) and alternative fuels for 39
sectors that are difficult to electrify (Section 6.6.2.4) – can lower the cost of decarbonization even with 40
very high shares of renewables (Figure 6.22). However, there is disagreement about the magnitude of 41
cost savings from larger portfolios, which depend on context- and scenario-specific assumptions about 42
technologies, markets, and policies.
