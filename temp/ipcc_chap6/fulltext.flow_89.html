Several end-uses such as passenger transportation (light-duty electric vehicles, two and three wheelers, 10
buses, rail) as well as building energy uses (lighting, cooling) are likely to be electrified in net zero 11
energy systems (high confidence). Variations in projections of electrification largely result from 12
differences in expectations about the ability and cost-competitiveness of electricity to serve other end 13
uses such as non-rail freight transport, aviation, and heavy industry (McCollum et al. 2014; Breyer et 14
al. 2019; Bataille et al. 2016; EPRI 2018) (Section 6.5.4.4), especially relative to biofuels and hydrogen 15
(‘low carbon fuels’) (Sachs et al. 2016; Rockström et al. 2017; McCollum et al. 2014), the prospects for 16
which are still quite uncertain (Section 6.4). The emergence of CDR technologies and the extent to 17
which they allow for residual emissions as an alternative to electrification will also affect the overall 18
share of energy served by electricity (Section 6.6.2.7). 19
Regions endowed with cheap and plentiful low-carbon electricity resources (wind, solar, hydropower) 20
are likely to emphasize electrification, while those with substantial bioenergy resources or availability 21
of other liquid fuels might put less emphasis on electrification, particularly in hard-to-electrify end-uses 22
(medium confidence). For example, among a group of Latin American countries, relative assumptions 23
about liquid fuels and electricity result in an electrification range of 28–82% for achieving a net zero 24
energy system (Bataille et al. 2020). Similarly, the level of penetration of biofuels that can substitute 25
for electrification will depend on regional circumstances such as land-use constraints, competition with 26
food, and sustainability of biomass production (Section 6.6.2.4). 27
Electrification of most buildings services, with the possible exception of space heating in extreme 28
climates, is expected in net zero energy systems (high confidence) (Chapter 9). Space cooling and water 29
heating are expected to be largely electrified. Building electrification is expected to rely substantially 30
on heat pumps, which will help lower emissions both through reduced thermal requirements and higher 31
efficiencies (Mathiesen et al. 2015; Rissman et al.; Sven et al. 2018). The level of electrification for 32
heating will depend on the tradeoffs between building or household level heat pumps versus more 33
centralized district heating network options (Mathiesen et al. 2015; Brown et al. 2018), as well as the 34
cost and performance of heat pumps in more extreme climates and grid infrastructure (EPRI 2018; 35
Waite and Modi 2020). 36
A significant share of transportation, especially road transportation, is expected to be electrified in net 37
zero energy systems (high confidence). In road transportation, two-three wheelers, light-duty vehicles 38
(LDVs), and buses, are especially amenable to electrification, with more than half of passenger LDVs 39
expected to be electrified globally in net zero energy systems (medium confidence) ((Bataille et al. 2020; 40
Sven et al. 2018; Khalili et al. 2019; Fulton et al. 2015). Long-haul trucks, large ships, and aircraft are 41
expected to be harder to electrify absent technological breakthroughs (Mathiesen et al. 2015; Fulton et 42
al. 2015), although continued improvements in battery technology may enable electrification of long-43
haul trucks (Nykvist and Olsson 2021; Chapter 10). Due to the relative ease of rail electrification, near 44
complete electrification of rail and a shift of air and truck freight to rail is expected in net zero energy 45
systems (Sven et al. 2018; Khalili et al. 2019; Rockström et al. 2017; Fulton et al. 2015). The degree of 46
modal shifts and electrification will depend on local factors such as infrastructure availability and 47
