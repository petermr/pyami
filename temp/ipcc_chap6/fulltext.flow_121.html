The literature discusses a broad set of policy approaches. Environmental economics focuses mainly on 4
market-based approaches as the least-cost policy to achieve emission reductions (Kube et al. 2018). 5
Many countries, however, have implemented policy mixes with a diverse set of complementary policies 6
to achieve energy and climate policy targets. One example is the German Energiewende, which includes 7
substantial support for renewables, an action plan for energy efficiency, and phase-out processes for 8
nuclear- and coal-based power generation next to carbon pricing (Löschel et al. 2019). The halving of 9
CO

emissions in UK power generation reflects multiple policies, particularly within the UK’s Climate 10
Change Act 2008 (Grubb and Newbery 2018). More generally, the implementation of the NDCs under 11
the Paris Agreement are all characterized by diverse climate policy mixes. 12
These policy mixes (or policy packages) are shaped by different factors, including policy goals and 13
objectives (including political, social and technological influences), multiple market, governance or 14
behavioural failures or previous policy choices of earlier policy eras (Rogge 2017). When pursuing 15
multiple policy goals or targeting some type of imperfection, well designed policy mixes can in 16
principle reduce mitigation costs (Corradini et al. 2018) or address distributional concerns, especially 17
vulnerable populations. For example, the interaction between carbon pricing and the support for clean 18
energy technologies in the EU clean low-carbon strategy for 2050 can reduce mitigation costs and allow 19
for the early adoption of more stringent climate targets (Vandyck et al. 2016). Policy efforts to promote 20
adoption of low-carbon technologies are more successful if they focus not only on economic incentives 21
but include behavioural interventions that target relevant cognitive and motivational factors (Khanna et 22
al. 2021; Mundaca et al. 2019; see also Section 6.7.6). Overlapping nudges might not necessarily lead 23
to lower effectiveness (Brandon et al. 2019). 24
Well-designed policy mixes can support the pursuit of multiple policy goals, target effectively different 25
types of imperfections and framework conditions and take into account the technological, economical, 26
and societal situation (high confidence). Accounting for the different development stages of new 27
technologies will enhance low-emissions transitions (Graaf and Sovacool 2020). For prototype 28
technologies and technologies in the demonstration phase, research subsidies and demonstration 29
projects are most important. For technologies experiencing early adoption, infrastructure development 30
and strengthening of markets are increasingly important, while retiring or repurposing of existing assets 31
is important for mature technologies (IEA 2020h) Effective policy mixes will address different market 32
frictions and deal with various uncertainties, for example, those pertaining to technological, climate, 33
and socio-economic developments (Aldy 2020), but also with respect to outcomes of individual policies 34
(e.g. Borenstein et al. 2019). Therefore, policy mixes may balance the trade-off between stability and 35
the flexibility to change individual policies (Gawel and Lehmann 2019) and the policy mix over time 36
(Rayner et al. 2017). Some policy instruments may become feasible over time, for example, as 37
technological advancements reduce the transaction costs of comprehensive market-based approaches 38
(Andoni et al. 2019; Di Silvestre et al. 2020), or as weakened barriers to stringency enable policy 39
sequencing (Pahle et al. 2018) . Energy system policy mixes often include sector-specific regulation. 40
Compared to economy-wide approaches, sectoral policies may be able to directly target specific sectors 41
or mitigation options. However, uncoordinated implementation or limited coordination across sectors 42
may lead to efficiency losses (e.g. Rosendahl et al. 2017). These losses also depend on other policies, 43
such as pre-existing taxes (Goulder et al. 2016; Marten et al. 2018) or research and development policies 44
(Acemoglu et al. 2016). Moreover, unilateral policies – those taken by individual countries in the 45
absence of coordination with other countries – could raise carbon leakage risks, while balancing 46
potential issues of (industrial) competitiveness (Martin et al. 2014; Rosendahl et al. 2017). Energy 47
