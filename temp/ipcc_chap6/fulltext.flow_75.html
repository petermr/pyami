6.5.2.2
Wind Energy 1
Climate change will not substantially impact future wind resources and will not compromise the ability 2
of wind energy to support low-carbon transitions (high confidence). Changing wind variability may 3
have a small to modest impact on backup energy and storage needs (low confidence); however, current 4
evidence is largely from studies focused on Europe. 5
Long-term global wind energy resources are not expected to substantially change in future climate 6
scenarios (Karnauskas et al. 2018; Yalew et al. 2020; Pryor et al. 2020). However, recent research has 7
indicated consistent shifts in the geographic position of atmospheric jets in the high emission scenarios 8
(Harvey et al. 2014), which would decrease wind power potentials across the Northern Hemisphere 9
mid-latitudes and increase wind potentials across the tropics and the Southern Hemisphere. However, 10
the climate models used to make these assessments differ in how well they can reproduce the historical 11
wind resources and wind extremes, which raises questions about the robustness of their predictions of 12
future wind resources (Pryor et al. 2020). 13
There are many regional studies on changes in wind resources from climate change. For Europe, there 14
is medium evidence and moderate agreement that wind resources are already increasing and will 15
continue to increase in Northern Europe and decrease in Southern Europe (Moemken et al. 2018; 16
Carvalho et al. 2017; Devis et al. 2018). For North America, the various studies have low agreement 17
for the changes in future wind resources in part because the year-to-year variations in wind resources 18
are often larger than the future change due to climate change (Johnson and Erhardt 2016; Wang et al. 19
2020b; Costoya et al. 2020; Chen 2020). Studies show increases in future wind resources in windy areas 20
in South America (Ruffato-Ferreira et al. 2017; de Jong et al. 2019). No robust future changes in wind 21
resources have been identified in China (Xiong et al. 2019). However, none of the global or regional 22
studies of the effects of climate change on wind resources considers the fine-scale dependence of wind 23
resources on the topography and wind direction (Sanz Rodrigo et al. 2016; Dörenkämper et al. 2020) 24
or the effect of expanding wind energy exploitation (Lundquist et al. 2019; Volker et al. 2017). There 25
is limited evidence that extreme wind speeds, which can damage wind turbines, will increase due to 26
climate change (Pes et al. 2017; Pryor et al. 2020). Nevertheless, projected changes in Europe and North 27
America – regions where the most extensive analysis has been undertaken – are expected to be within 28
the estimates embedded in the design standards of wind turbines (Pryor and Barthelmie 2013). 29
Future wind generation in Europe could decrease in summer and autumn, increasing in winter in 30
northern-central Europe but decreasing in southernmost Europe (Carvalho et al. 2017). Towards 2100, 31
intra-annual variations increase in most of Europe, except around the Mediterranean area (Reyers et al. 32
2016), but this may reflect natural multidecadal variability (Wohland et al. 2019b). Wind speeds may 33
become more homogeneous over large geographical regions in Europe due to climate change, 34
increasing the likelihood of large areas experiencing high or low wind speeds simultaneously (Wohland 35
et al. 2017). These changes could result in fewer benefits in the transmission of wind generation between 36
countries and increased system integration costs. Europe could require a modest increase (up to 7%) in 37
backup energy towards the end of the 21
st
century due to more homogeneous wind conditions over 38
Europe (Wohland et al. 2017; Weber et al. 2018). However, other studies report that impact of climate 39
change is substantially smaller than interannual variability, with no significant impact on the occurrence 40
of extreme low wind production events in Europe (Van Der Wiel et al. 2019). If European electricity 41
systems are designed to manage the effects of existing weather variability on wind power, they can 42
likely also cope with climate change impacts on wind power (Ravestein et al. 2018). Changes in wind 43
generation variability caused by climate change are also reported for North America (Haupt et al. 2016; 44
Losada Carreño et al. 2018), with modest impacts on electricity system operation (Craig et al. 2019). 45
