Solar Energy 1
Climate change is not expected to substantially impact global solar insolation and will not compromise 2
the ability of solar energy to support low-carbon transitions (high confidence). Models show dimming 3
and brightening in certain regions, driven by cloud, aerosol and water vapour trends [WGI,ch12,p31]. 4
The increase in surface temperature, which affects all regions, decreases solar power output by reducing 5
the PV panel efficiency. In some models and climate scenarios, the increases in solar insolation are 6
counterbalanced by reducing efficiency due to rising surface air temperatures, which increase 7
significantly in all models and scenarios (Jerez et al. 2015; Bartók et al. 2017; Emodi et al. 2019). 8
Increases in aerosols would reduce the solar resource available and add to maintenance costs 9
[AR6,WGI,ch12]. 10
In many emission scenarios, the effect on solar PV from temperature-induced efficiency losses is 11
smaller than the effect expected from changes on solar insolation due to variations in water vapour and 12
clouds in most regions. Also, future PV technologies will likely have higher efficiency, which would 13
offset temperature-related declines (Müller et al. 2019). Cloud cover is projected to decrease in the 14
subtropics (around –0.05% per year), including parts of North America, vast parts of Europe and China, 15
South America, South Africa and Australia (medium agreement, medium evidence). Thus, models 16
project modest (< 3%) increases in solar PV by the end of the century for southern Europe, northern 17
and southern Africa, Central America, and the Caribbean (Emodi et al. 2019). There are several studies 18
projecting decreasing solar production, but these are generally influenced by other factors, for example, 19
increasing air pollution (Ruosteenoja et al. 2019). The multi-model means for solar insolation in 20
regional models decrease 0.60 W m

Bioenergy 23
Climate change can affect biomass resource potential directly, via changes in the suitable range (i.e., 24
the area where bioenergy crops can grow) and/or changes in yield, and indirectly, through changes in 25
land availability. Increases in CO
2
concentration increase biomass yield; climate changes (e.g., 26
temperature, precipitation, etc.) can either increase or decrease the yield and suitable range. 27
Climate change will shift the suitable range for bioenergy towards higher latitudes, but the net change 28
in the total suitable area is uncertain (high confidence). Several studies show northward shifts in the 29
suitable range for bioenergy in the northern hemisphere (Tuck et al. 2006; Bellarby et al. 2010; Preston 30
et al. 2016; Barney and DiTomaso 2010; Hager et al. 2014; Conant et al. 2018; Cronin et al. 2018; 31
Wang et al. 2014a), but the net effect of climate change on total suitable area varies by region, species, 32
and climate model (Barney and DiTomaso 2010; Hager et al. 2014; Wang et al. 2014a). 33
The effect of climate change on bioenergy crop yields will vary across region and feedstock (high 34
confidence); however, in general, yields will decline in low latitudes (medium confidence) and increase 35
in high latitudes (low confidence) (Haberl et al. 2010; Cosentino et al. 2012; Mbow et al. 2019; Cronin 36
et al. 2018; Preston et al. 2016). However, the average change in yield varies significantly across studies, 37
depending on the feedstock, region, and other factors (Dolan et al. 2020; Kyle et al. 2014) Mbow et al. 38
(2019); Beringer et al. (2011). Only a few studies extend the modelling of climate change impacts on 39
bioenergy to quantify the effect on bioenergy deployment or its implications on the energy system 40
(Calvin et al. 2013, 2019; Thornton et al. 2017; Kyle et al. 2014). These studies find that changes in 41
deployment are of the same sign as changes in yield; that is, if yields increase, then deployment 42
increases. 43
