Redox Flow Batteries (RFB). Redox flow batteries use two separate electrolyte solutions, usually 3
liquids, but solid or gaseous forms may also be involved, stored in separate tanks, and pumped over or 4
through electrode stacks during charge and discharge, with an ion-conducting membrane separating the 5
liquids. The larger the tank, the greater the energy storage capacity, whereas more and larger cells in 6
the stack increase the power of the flow battery. This decoupling of energy from power enables RFB 7
installations to be uniquely tailored to suit the requirements of any given application. There are two 8
commercially available types today: vanadium and zinc bromide, and both operate at near ambient 9
temperatures, incurring minimal operational costs. 10
RFBs respond rapidly and can perform all the same services as LIBs, except for onboard electricity for 11
EVs. Lower cost chemistries are emerging, to enable cost-effective bulk energy storage (Brandon et al. 12
2015). A new membrane-free design eliminates the need for a separator and also halves the system 13
requirements, as the chemical reactions can coexist in a single electrolyte solution (Navalpotro et al. 14
2017; Arenas et al. 2018). 15
Power to fuels (PtX). (see also Section 6.4.3.1) The process of using electricity to generate a gaseous 16
fuel, such as hydrogen or ammonia, is termed power-to-gas (PtG/P2G) (IEA 2020h). When injected 17
into the existing gas infrastructure (section 6.4.5), it has the added benefit of decarbonizing gas 18
(Brandon et al. 2015). Electricity can be used to generate hydrogen, which is then converted back into 19
electricity using combined-cycle gas turbines that have been converted to run on hydrogen. For greater 20
compatibility with existing gas systems and appliances, the hydrogen can combined with captured 21
carbon dioxide to form methane and other synthetic fuels (Thema et al. 2019), however methane has 22
high global warming potential and its supply chain emissions have been found to be significant 23
(Balcombe et al. 2013). 24
PtX can provide all required grid services, depending on how it is integrated. However, a significant 25
amount of PtX is required for storage to produce electricity again (Bogdanov et al. 2019) due to the low 26
roundtrip efficiency of converting electricity to fuel and back again. However, portable fuels (hydrogen, 27
methane, ammonia, synthetic hydrocarbons) are useful in certain applications, for example in energy 28
systems lacking the potential for renewables, and the high energy density of chemical storage is 29
essential for more demanding applications, such as transporting heavy goods and heating or cooling 30
buildings (IEA 2020h). Research into more efficient and flexible electrolyzers which last longer and 31
cost less is needed (Brandon et al. 2015). 32
Hydrogen and Reversible Hydrogen Fuel Cells (H/RHFC). Hydrogen is a flexible fuel with diverse 33
uses, capable of providing electricity, heat, and long-term energy storage for grids, industry, and 34
transport, and has been widely used industrially for decades (Section 0). Hydrogen can be produced in 35
various ways and stored in significant quantities in geological formations at moderate pressures, often 36
for long periods, providing seasonal storage (Gabrielli et al. 2020). A core and emerging 37
implementation of PtX is hydrogen production through electrolyzers. Hydrogen is a carbon-free fuel 38
holding three times the energy held by an equivalent mass of gasoline but occupying a larger volume. 39
An electrolyzer uses excess electricity to split water into hydrogen and oxygen through the process of 40
electrolysis. A fuel cell performs the reverse process of recombining hydrogen and oxygen back into 41
water, converting chemical energy into electricity (Elzinga et al. 2014). Reversible hydrogen fuel cells 42
(RHFCs) can perform both functions in a single device, however they are still in the pre-commercial 43
stage, due to prohibitive production costs. 44
