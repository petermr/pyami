Between 2015 and 2019, global coal power capacity grew by 146 GW, or 7.6%, as new builds offset 12
retirements in some countries (Global Energy Monitor et al. 2021). Meanwhile, an increasing number 13
of countries and regions have committed to or operationalised coal phase-outs (Jewell et al. 2019; Watts 14
et al. 2019; Littlecott et al. 2021). Actions are being taken by various international and subnational 15
actors, including national and subnational governments, public and private power companies, and 16
financial institutions and pension funds that have committed not to fund new coal or coal-based 17
infrastructure (yan Nie et al. 2016; Buckley 2019; Auger et al. 2021). Although these initial efforts are 18
not yet sufficient in limiting warming to 1.5°C, and most have occurred in regions with older coal fleets, 19
these examples provide insight into possible coal phaseout strategies (Spencer et al. 2018) and help 20
identify the mechanisms driving the move away from coal, such as market, technology, policy, or other 21
societal objectives. They also enable better understanding of the possible character of oil and gas phase-22
downs that would ultimately be needed to limit warming two well below 2°C (Section 6.7.4; (Raimi et 23
