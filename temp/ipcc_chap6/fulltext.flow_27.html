Another environmental concern with large PV power plants is the conversion of land to collect solar 21
energy (Hernandez et al. 2015). Approximately 2 hectares of land are needed for 1 MW of solar 22
electricity capacity (Kabir et al. 2018; Perpiña Castillo et al. 2016); at 20% efficiency, a square of PV 23
panels of 550 km by 550 km, comprising 0.2% of Earth’s land area, could meet global energy demand. 24
Land conversion can have local impacts, especially near cities and where land used for solar competes 25
with alternative uses, such as agriculture. Large installations can also adversely impact biodiversity 26
(Hernandez et al. 2014), especially where the above ground vegetation is cleared and soils are typically 27
graded. Landscape fragmentation creates barriers to the movement of species. However, a variety of 28
means have emerged to mitigate land use issues. Substitution among renewables can reduce land 29
conversion (Tröndle and Trondle 2020). Solar can be integrated with other uses through ‘agrivoltaics’ 30
(the use of land for both agriculture and solar production) (Dupraz et al. 2011) by, for example, using 31
shade-tolerant crops (Dinesh and Pearce 2016). Combining solar and agriculture can also create income 32
diversification, reduced drought stress, higher solar output due to radiative cooling, and other benefits 33
(Elamri et al. 2018; Hassanpour Adeh et al. 2018; Barron-Gafford et al. 2019). PV installations floating 34
on water also avoid land-use conflicts (Sahu et al. 2016; Lee et al. 2020), as does dual-use infrastructure, 35
such as landfills (Jäger-Waldau 2020) and reservoirs where evaporation can also be reduced (Farfan 36
and Breyer 2018). 37
Material demand for PV will likely increase substantially to limit warming to well below 2°C, but PV 38
materials are widely available, have possible substitutes, and can be recycled (medium confidence) (Box 39
6.4). The primary materials for PV are silicon, copper, glass, aluminium, and silver, the costliest being 40
silicon and glass being the most essential by mass, at 70%. None of these materials is considered to be 41
either critical or potentially scarce (IEA 2020e). Thin-film cells, such as amorphous silicon, cadmium 42
telluride and copper indium gallium diselenide (CIGS), use far less material (though they use more 43
glass), but account for less than 10% of the global solar market. Other thin-films, such as those based 44
on perovskites, organic solar cells, or earth-abundant, non-toxic materials such as kesterites, either on 45
their own, or layered on silicon, could further reduce material use per energy produced. (Box 6.4) 46
