Energy storage technologies can provide a range of different grid services (Table 6.5). Energy storage 2
enhances security of supply by providing real time system regulation services (voltage support, 3
frequency regulation, fast reserve, and short-term reserve). A greater proportion of variable renewable 4
sources reduces system inertia, requiring more urgent responses to changes in system frequency, which 5
rapid response storage technologies can provide (stability requires responses within sub second 6
timescale for provision of frequency and voltage control services). Energy storage also provides 7
intermittent renewable sources with flexibility, allowing them to contribute a greater proportion of 8
electrical energy and avoiding curtailment (capacity firming). Investment costs in backup generation, 9
interconnection, transmission, and distribution network upgrades can thus be reduced (upgrade 10
deferral), meaning that less low carbon generation will need to be built while still reducing emissions. 11
In the event of an outage, energy storage reserves can keep critical services running (islanding) and 12
restart the grid (black start). The ability to store and release energy as required provides a range of 13
market opportunities for buying and selling of energy (arbitrage). 14
No single, sufficiently mature energy storage technology can provide all the required grid services - a 15
portfolio of complementary technologies working together can provide the optimum solution (high 16
confidence). Different energy storage technologies can provide these services and support cost-effective 17
energy system decarbonization (Carbon Trust 2016). To achieve very low carbon systems, significant 18
volumes of storage will be required (Strbac et al. 2015a; Section 0). There are few mature global supply 19
chains for many of the less-developed energy storage technologies. This means that although costs 20
today may be relatively high, there are significant opportunities for future cost reductions, both through 21
technology innovation and through manufacturing scale. Adding significant amounts of storage will 22
reduce the price variation and, therefore, the profitability of additional and existing storage, increasing 23
investment risk. 24
Energy storage extends beyond electricity storage and includes technologies that can store energy as 25
heat, cold, and both liquid and gaseous fuels. Energy storage is a conversion technology, enabling 26
energy to be converted from one form to another. This diversification improves the overall resilience 27
of energy systems, with each system being able to cover supply shortfalls in the others. For example, 28
storage can support the electrification of heating or cooling, as well as transport through electric 29
vehicles, powered by batteries or by fuel cells. Storage significantly reduces the need for costly 30
reinforcement of local distribution networks through smart charging schemes and the ability to flow 31
electricity back to the grid (e.g., through vehicle-to-grid). By capturing otherwise wasted energy 32
streams, such as heat or cold, energy storage improves the efficiency of many systems, such as 33
buildings, data centres and industrial processes. 34

the medium. Water is pumped into an elevated reservoir using off-peak electricity and stored for later 37
