Interactions between policy measures including their scope, stringency, and timing, influence the costs 6
of reducing emissions (Corradini et al. 2018). In particular, some policy instruments may lead to lock-7
in effects (Section 6.7.3.), compete with other regulations (Graaf and Sovacool 2020), or trigger 8
negative policy interactions (Perino 2015; Jarke-Neuert and Perino 2020). Existing policy mixes often 9
reflect different political economy constraints and sometimes not well coordinated goals. The resulting 10
policy mixes are often economically inefficient. However, comprehensive evaluation of policy mixes 11
requires a broader set of criteria that reflect different considerations, such as broader goals (e.g., SDGs) 12
and the feasibility of policies (high confidence). 13
Policy mixes might rather emerge piece-by-piece over time out of individual policy interventions rather 14
than be designed as a whole from the outset (Howlett 2014; Rogge 2017) and may reflect differences 15
across jurisdictions and sectors (Howlett 2014). For example, taking into account country-specific 16
objectives, failures, and limitations, carbon prices may be only one part of a broader policy mix and 17
thereby may not be uniform across countries (Bataille 2020). This lack of consistency makes it more 18
difficult to assess economic outcomes since costs of complementary policies are often less visible and 19
are often targeted at high-cost mitigation options (Borenstein et al. 2019). 20
Effective assessment of policy mixes requires comprehensive, validated international data, 21
methodologies, and indicators. Existing policy mixes are difficult to evaluate because they target 22
multiple objectives, and the evaluation must consider various criteria (Chapter 13, 6.7.7), such as 23
environmental and economic effectiveness, distributional effects, transformative potential, institutional 24
requirements, and feasibility. Economic outcomes depend on policy goals and implementation. Existing 25
studies on policy mixes suggest the benefits of a comprehensive approach (Rosenow et al. 2017), while 26
also highlighting that an “excessive” number of instruments may reduce overall effectiveness 27
(Costantini et al. 2017). Combining environmental regulation and innovation policies may be of 28
particular importance to tackle both emissions and innovation market failures (Fabrizi et al. 2018). The 29
consistency and credibility of policy mixes is positively associated with green innovation (Rogge and 30
Schleich 2018). 31
Potential future policies are difficult to evaluate due to methodological challenges (high confidence). 32
Recent model-based analyses of future policy mixes based on “current policy scenarios” try to 33
implement existing policies besides explicit or implicit carbon prices (den Elzen et al. 2016; Roelfsema 34
et al. 2020; van Soest et al. 2017; Rogelj et al. 2016). Many assessments of future low-carbon energy 35
transitions are still based on cost-optimal evaluation frameworks and include only limited analysis of 36
interactions between policy measures. Hence they are often not describing real-world energy transitions 37
properly, but rather differences in implied carbon prices, constraints in technology deployment, and 38
timing of policies (Trutnevyte 2016). 39

Members of societies, including individuals, civil society, and businesses, will all need to engage with 42
and be affected by low-carbon energy system transitions (high confidence). This raises questions about 43
the extent to which different strategies and policy would effectively promote mitigation behaviours and 44
the factors that increase the social acceptability of mitigation options, policies, and system changes. 45
