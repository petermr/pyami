Box 6.13 Stranded Assets 6
Limiting likely warming to 2°C or below will result in stranded assets (high confidence). Stranded assets 7
can be broadly defined as assets which “suffer from unanticipated or premature write-offs, downward 8
revaluations or [conversion] to liabilities.” Stranded assets may create risks for financial market stability 9
and macro-economic stability (Mercure et al. 2018, Battiston et al. 2017; Sen and von Schickfus 2020), 10
and they will result in a rapid loss of wealth for the owners of affected assets (Vogt-Schilb and 11
Hallegatte 2017; Ploeg and Rezai 2020). 12
There are two types of stranded assets: fossil-fuel resources that cannot be burned, and premature 13
retirement of fossil infrastructure (e.g., power plants). About 30% of oil, 50% of gas, and 80% of coal 14
reserves will remain unburnable if likely warming is limited to 2°C (Meinshausen et al. 2009; Leaton 15
2011; Leaton Ranger 2013; Pye et al. 2020; IRENA 2017b; Bauer et al. 2016; McGlade and Ekins 2015) 16
(high confidence). Significantly more reserves are expected to remain unburned if warming is limited 17
to 1.5°C. Countries with large oil, gas, and coal reserves are most at risk (Caldecott et al. 2017; Ansari 18
and Holz 2020) 19
About 200 GW of fossil fuel electricity generation per year will likely need to be retired prematurely 20
after 2030 to limit likely warming to 2°C, even if countries achieve their NDCs (Iyer et al. 2015; 21
Johnson et al. 2015; Fofrich et al. 2020) (medium confidence). Limiting warming to 1.5°C will require 22
significantly more rapid premature retirement of electricity generation capacity (Binsted et al. 2020). 23
Coal- and gas-fired power plants will likely need to retire about 25 years earlier than in the past to limit 24
likely warming to 2°C, and 30 years earlier to limit warming to 1.5°C (Cui et al. 2019; Fofrich et al. 25
2020). Coal-fired power plants are at significantly greater risk of stranding compared with gas-fired and 26
oil-fired plants (Iyer et al. 2015; Johnson et al. 2015; Fofrich et al. 2020). The risks of stranded power 27
plants are greatest in countries with newer fossil infrastructure. 28
If likely warming is limited to 2°C, the discounted economic impacts of stranded assets, including 29
unburned fossil reserves, could be as high as USD 1-4 trillion from 2015 through 2050 (USD 10-20 30
trillion in undiscounted terms) (Mercure et al. 2018), IRENA, 2017) (medium confidence). About 40% 31
of these impacts correspond to unburned fossil reserves (IRENA 2017b). If warming is limited to 1.5°C, 32
the economic impacts of stranded assets are expected to be significantly higher (Binsted et al. 2020) 33
Stronger near-term mitigation will reduce premature retirements of fossil infrastructure, because more 34
rapid mitigation will decrease new builds of fossil infrastructure that might later be stranded (Johnson 35
et al. 2015; Bertram et al. 2018) (high confidence). For example, if likely warming is limited to 2°C, 36
strengthening the NDC pledges beyond their 2015 levels could decrease stranded electricity sector 37
assets by more than 50% (Iyer et al. 2015). By contrast, if countries fail to meet their NDCs and continue 38
to build fossil infrastructure, mitigation will need to be accelerated beyond 2030, resulting up to double 39
the amount of stranded electricity generation capacity (Iyer et al. 2015). This corresponds to a total 40
undiscounted cost of about USD 2 trillion from electricity infrastructure alone, from the period 2015 41
to 2050 (IRENA 2017). CCS (6.4) could potentially help reduce hundreds of gigawatts stranded power 42
plant capacity along with other fossil-based capital (Clark and Herzog 2014; Fan et al. 2018; Iyer et al. 43
2017). 44
