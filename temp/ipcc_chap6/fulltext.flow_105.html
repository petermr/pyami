6.7.1.2
Low-carbon energy transition strategies 1
There are multiple technological routes to reduce energy system emissions (see Section 6.6). Here we 2
discuss three of these: (1) decarbonizing primary energy and electricity generation, (2) switching to 3
electricity, bioenergy, hydrogen, and other fuels produced from low-carbon sources, and (3) limiting 4
energy use through improvement of efficiency and conservation. CDR is discussed in Section 6.7.1.X; 5
Fossil fuel transitions are discussed in Section 6.7.4. 6
Decarbonizing Primary Energy and Electricity Generation. Limiting warming to well below 2°C 7
requires a rapid and dramatic increase in energy produced from low- or zero-carbon sources (high 8
confidence). Low- and zero-carbon technologies produce 74-82% (interquartile range) of primary 9
energy in 2050 in scenarios limiting warming to 1.5°C with no or limited overshoot and 55-68% in 10
likely below 2°C scenarios (Figure 6.29). The share of low-carbon technologies in global primary 11
energy supply today is below 20% (Section 6.3, Chapter 3,Figure 6.29). The percentage of low- and 12
zero-carbon energy will depend in part on the evolution of energy demand – the more that energy 13
demand grows, the more energy from low- and zero-carbon sources will be needed and the higher the 14
percentage of total primary energy these sources will represent. 15
Low- and zero-carbon sources produce 97-99% of global electricity in 2050 in scenarios limiting 16
warming to 1.5°C with no or limited overshoot and 93-97% in likely below 2°C scenarios (Figure 6.29) 17
(medium confidence). Decarbonizing electricity generation, in tandem with increasing use of electricity 18
(see below), is an essential near-term strategy for limiting warming. The increase in low- and zero-19
carbon electricity will occur while electricity demand grows substantially. Studies have projected that 20
global electricity demand will roughly double by 2050 and quadruple to quintuple by 2100 irrespective 21
of efforts to reduce emissions (Bauer et al. 2017; Luderer et al. 2017; IEA 2019a). 22
Renewable energy, especially generation from solar and wind, is likely to have an important role in 23
many low-carbon electricity systems. The contributions of wind and solar electricity will depend on 24
their levelized costs relative to other options, integration costs, system value, and the ability to integrate 25
variable resources into the grid (Section 6.6). Electric sector technology mixes will vary by region but 26
will typically include additional resources such as hydropower, nuclear power, fossil generation with 27
CCS, energy storage resources, and geothermal energy, among others. Contributions of different 28
options vary widely across scenarios based on different assumptions about these factors (Figure 6.30). 29
Nonetheless, it is likely that wind and solar will dominate low-carbon generation and capacity growth 30
over the next couple decades due to supporting policies in many countries and due to their significant 31
roles in early electric sector decarbonization, alongside reductions in coal generation (Bistline and 32
Blanford 2021b; Pan et al. 2021). Clean firm technologies play important roles in providing flexibility 33
and on-demand generation for longer durations, though deployment of these technologies is typically 34
associated with deeper decarbonization levels (e.g., beyond 70-80% reductions), which are likely to be 35
more important after 2030 in many regions, and with more limited CDR deployment (Baik et al. 2021; 36
Williams et al. 2021a; Bistline and Blanford 2021a). 37
