6.6.2.5
Using Less Energy and Using It More Efficiently 4
Demand-side or demand reduction strategies include technology efficiency improvements, strategies 5
that reduce energy consumption or demand for energy services (such as reducing the use of personal 6
transportation, often called “conservation”) (Creutzig et al. 2018), and strategies such as load 7
curtailment. 8
Net-zero energy systems will use energy more efficiently than those of today (high confidence). Energy 9
efficiency and energy use reduction strategies are generally identified as being flexible and cost-10
effective, with the potential for large scale deployment (Chapters 5, 9, 10, and 11). For this reason, 11
existing studies find that energy efficiency and demand reduction strategies will be important 12
contributors to net zero energy systems (Creutzig et al. 2018; Davis et al. 2018; DeAngelo et al. 2021). 13
Lower demand reduces the need for low-carbon energy or alternative fuel sources. 14
Characterizing efficiency of net zero energy systems is problematic due to measurement challenges 15
(high confidence). Efficiency itself is difficult to define and measure across full economies (Saunders 16
et al. 2021). There is no single definition of energy efficiency and the definition understandably depends 17
on the context used (Patterson 1996), which ranges from device level efficiency all the way to the 18
efficient use of energy throughout an economy. Broadly, energy efficient strategies allow for the same 19
level of services or output while using less energy. At the level of the entire economy, measures such 20
as primary or final energy per capita or per GDP are often used as a proxy for energy efficiency, but 21
these measures reflect not only efficiency, but also many other factors such as industrial structure, 22
endowed natural resources, consumer preferences, policies, and regulations. Energy efficiency and 23
other demand-side strategies represent such a large set of technologies, strategies, policies, market and 24
consumers’ responses and policies that aggregate measures can be difficult to define (Saunders et al. 25
2021). 26
Measurement issues notwithstanding, virtually all studies that address net zero energy systems assume 27
improved energy intensity in the future (high confidence). The overall efficiency outcomes and the 28
access to such improvements across different nations, however, is not clear. Energy consumption will 29
increase over time despite energy efficiency improvements due to population growth and development 30
(DeAngelo et al. 2021). 31
A study (DeAngelo et al. 2021) reviewed 153 IAM scenarios that attain net zero energy sector CO2 32
emissions and found that, under a scenario with net zero emissions: global final energy per capita lies 33
between 21–109 GJ per person (median: 57), in comparison to 2018 global final energy use of 55 GJ 34
per person; many countries use far more energy per capita than today as their incomes increase; global 35
final energy use per unit of economic output ranges from 0.7–2.2 EJ per trillion USD (median: 1.5), in 36
comparison to 5 EJ per trillion USD in 2018; and the median final energy consumption is 529 EJ. By 37
comparison, final energy consumption would be 550 EJ if current energy consumption per capita 38
continued under a future population of 10 billion people. Across all scenarios, total final energy 39
consumption is higher today than in the year in which net zero emissions are attained, and regionally, 40
only the OECD+EU and Eurasia have lower median total final energy than in 2010. 41
