Importance of cross-sector coupling for cost-effective energy system decarbonization 3
Integrated whole-system approaches can reduce the costs of low-carbon energy system transitions (high 4
confidence). A lack of flexibility in the electricity system may limit the cost-effective integration of 5
technologies as part of broader net zero energy systems. At the same time, the enormous latent 6
flexibility hidden in heating and cooling, hydrogen, transport, gas systems, and other energy systems 7
provides opportunities to take advantage of synergies and to coordinate operations across systems 8
(Martinez Cesena and Mancarella 2019; Zhang et al. 2018; Bogdanov et al. 2021; Pavičević et al. 2020; 9
Martin et al. 2017) (Figure 6.16). 10
Sector coupling can significantly increase system flexibility, driven by the application of advanced 11
technologies (Bogdanov et al. 2019; Solomon et al. 2019; Clegg and Mancarella 2016; Zhang and 12
Fujimori 2020; Zhao et al. 2021; Heinen et al. 2016; Zhang et al. 2019b). For example, district heating 13
infrastructure can generate both heat and power. Cooling systems and electrified heating systems in 14
buildings can provide flexibility through preheating and precooling via thermal energy storage Li, G. 15
et al. 2017; Li, Z. et al. 2016).. System balancing services can be provided by electric vehicles (EVs) 16
based on vehicle-to-grid concepts and deferred charging through smart control of EV batteries without 17
compromising customers’ requirements for transport (Aunedi and Strbac 2020). 18
Hydrogen production processes (power-to-gas and vice versa) and hydrogen storage can support short-19
term and long-term balancing in the energy systems and enhance resilience (Stephen and Pierluigi 2016; 20
Strbac et al. 2020). However, the economic benefits of flexible power-to-gas plants, energy storage, 21
and other flexibility technological and options will depend on the locations of VRE sources, storage 22
sites, gas, hydrogen, and electricity networks (Jentsch et al. 2014; Heymann and Bessa 2015; Ameli et 23
al. 2020). Coordinated operation of gas and electricity systems can bring significant benefits in 24
supplying heat demands. For example, hybrid heating can eliminate investment in electricity 25
infrastructure reinforcement by switching to heat pumps in off-peak hours and gas boilers in peak hours 26
( Dengiz et al. 2019; Fischer et al. 2017; Bistline et al. 2021). The heat required by direct air carbon 27
capture and storage (DACCS) could be effectively supplied by inherent heat energy in nuclear plants, 28
enhancing overall system efficiency (Realmonte et al. 2019). 29
