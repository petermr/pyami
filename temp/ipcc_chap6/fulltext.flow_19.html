North America: Coal use has been declining in North America. In the U.S., the primary driver has 11
been the availability of cheap shale gas and ageing coal fleets. Coal use in the US has dropped by over 12
50% since 2008 (EIA 2019). The recently announced NDC by the Biden Administration sets a 100% 13
carbon-free electricity goal by 2035 (The White House 2021), indicating a phaseout not only of 14
unabated coal electricity generation, but also of natural gas generation. As one of the two founding 15
countries of the PPCA, Canada has committed to phasing out unabated coal power by 2030 16
(Government of Canada 2018). Declining coal use in both the U.S. and Canada has decreased GHG 17
emissions, local air pollutants, and cooling water use (Harris et al. 2015; Kondash et al. 2019). However, 18
there have been concerns about social and economic consequences, particularly at the local level. For 19
instance, the U.S. has lost about 50,000 coal mining jobs between 2011 and 2021 (U.S. Bureau of Labor 20
Statistics, 2021), with significant regional and economic inequities (Bodenhamer 2016; Abraham 2017; 21
Greenberg 2018). Comprehensive social programs, such as retirement compensation, training for 22
reemployment, and business support for economic diversification, have been suggested as means to 23
support a just transition (Patrizio et al. 2018; Homagain et al. 2015; Grubert 2020). 24

and retired a small set of old, dirty, inefficient coal plants (Global Energy Monitor et al. 2021; CEA 29
2019). These efforts are largely due to non-climate reasons, such air pollution and health (Singh and 30
Rao 2015; Gass et al. 2016; Peng et al. 2018; Malik et al. 2020), overcapacity (Blondeel and Van de 31
Graaf 2018), and rural electrification and renewable investments (Aklin et al. 2017; Thapar et al. 2018). 32
However, as new builds offset retirements, coal generation capacity has continued to grow in both 33
countries since 2015 (Global Energy Monitor et al. 2021). Other fast-growing Southeast Asian 34
countries, such as Indonesia, Vietnam, and Philippines have experienced strong growth in coal use (IEA 35
2020b), but an increasing number of new coal power projects are being cancelled (Littlecott et al. 2021). 36
Coal projects in these countries are decreasingly likely to proceed because they rely on international 37
financing, and China, Japan, United States, and other G7 countries have pledged to end overseas coal 38
financing (Schiermeier 2021). 39
Africa: New coal power projects in Africa have been declining since 2016, with only South Africa and 40
Zimbabwe currently building new coal plants and several others has planned projects (Littlecott et al. 41
2021). However, these projects also largely depend on international financing and are thus less likely 42
to be implemented (see above). In South Africa, employment in the coal mining sector has dropped by 43
almost half since the 1980s and is has been estimated to fall from 77,00 today to 22,000 to 42,000 by 44
2050 (Strambo et al. 2019; Cock 2019). Policy and financial support are essential to ensure a sustainable 45
