Challenges, barriers, and recommendations. The main challenge to inter-regional transmission is the 15
absence of appropriate market designs and regulatory and policy frameworks. In addition, there are 16
commercial barriers for further enhancement of cross-border transmission. The differing impacts of 17
cross-border interconnections on costs and revenues for generation companies in different regions could 18
delay the development of these interconnectors. It is not yet clear how the investment cost of 19
interconnections should be allocated and recovered, although there is growing support for allocating 20
costs in accordance with the benefits delivered to the market participants. Increased cross-border 21
interconnection may also require new business models which provide incentives for investment and 22
efficient operation, manage risks and uncertainties, and facilitate coordinated planning and governance 23
(Poudineh and Rubino 2017). 24
Optimizing the design and operation of the interconnected transmission system, both onshore and 25
offshore grids, also requires more integrated economic and reliability approaches (Moreno et al. 2012) 26
to ensure the optimal balance between the economics and the provision of system security while 27
maximizing the benefits of smart network technologies. 28
A wide range of factors, including generation profiles, demand profiles circuit losses, reliability 29
characteristics, and maintenance, as well as the uncertainties around them will need to be considered in 30
designing and operating long-distance transmission systems if they are to be widely deployed (De Sa 31
and Al Zubaidy 2011; Du 2009; Djapic et al. 2008; E3G 2021). Public support for extending 32
transmission systems will also be crucial, and studies indicate that such support is frequently low 33
(Perlaviciute et al. 2018; Vince 2010). 34

Demand-side measures are fundamental to an integrated approach to low carbon energy systems (high 36
confidence). Mitigation options, such as wind parks, CCS, and nuclear power plants, may not be 37
implemented when actors oppose these options. Further, end users, including consumers, governments, 38
businesses and industry, would need to adopt the relevant options, and then use these as intended; user 39
adoption can be a key driver to scale up markets for low carbon technologies. This section discusses 40
which factors shape the likelihood that end users engage in relevant mitigation actions, focusing on 41
consumers; strategies to promote mitigation actions are discussed in Section 6.7.6.1. 42
