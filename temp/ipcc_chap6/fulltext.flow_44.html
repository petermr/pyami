Liquid Biofuels. First-generation sugar-based biofuels (e.g., ethanol produced via fermentation) or 6
biodiesel produced from vegetable oils and animal fats are produced in several countries at large 7
scale and costs competitive with fossil fuels. However, supply is limited. The costs for second 8
generation processes (Fischer-Tropsch and cellulosic ethanol) are higher in most regions (Li et al. 9

Large-scale bioenergy production will require more than wastes/residues and cultivation on marginal 11
lands, which may raise conflicts with SDGs relevant to environmental and societal priorities (Gerten et 12
al. 2020; Heck et al. 2018) (Chapter 12). These include competition with food crops, implications for 13
biodiversity, potential deforestation to support bioenergy crop production, energy security implications 14
from bioenergy trade, point-of-use emissions and associated effects on air quality, and water use and 15
fertilizer use (Fajardy and Mac Dowell 2018; Tanzer and Ramírez 2019; Fuss et al. 2018; Brack and 16
King 2020). Overall, the environmental impact of bioenergy production at scale remains uncertain and 17
varies by region and application. 18
Alleviating these issues would require some combination of increasing crop yields, improving 19
conversion efficiencies, and developing advanced biotechnologies for increasing the fuel yield per 20
tonne of feedstock (Henry et al. 2018). Policy structures would be necessary to retain biodiversity, 21
manage water use, limit deforestation and land-use change emissions, and ultimately optimally integrate 22
bioenergy with transforming ecosystems. Large-scale international trade of biomass might be required 23
to support a global bioeconomy, raising questions about infrastructure, logistics, financing options, and 24
global standards for bioenergy production and trade (Box 6.10). Additional institutional and economic 25
barriers are associated with accounting of carbon dioxide removal, including BECCS (Fuss et al. 2014; 26
Muratori et al. 2016; Fridahl and Lehtveer 2018). 27
Life-cycle emissions impacts from bioenergy are subject to large uncertainties and could be 28
incompatible with net zero emissions in some contexts. Due to the potentially large energy conversion 29
requirements and associated GHG emissions (Chapter 7, Chapter 12), bioenergy systems may fail to 30
deliver near-zero emissions depending on operating conditions and regional contexts (Staples et al. 31
2017; Lade et al. 2020; Daioglou et al. 2017; Hanssen et al. 2020; Elshout et al. 2015). As a result, 32
bioenergy carbon neutrality is debated and depends on factors such as the source of biomass, conversion 33
pathways and energy used for production and transport of biomass, and land use changes, as well as 34
assumed analysis boundary and considered timescale (Fan et al. 2021; Wiloso et al. 2016; Zanchi et al. 35
2012; Booth 2018). Similarly, the lifecycle emissions of BECCS remain uncertain and will depend on 36
how effectively bioenergy conversion processes are optimized (Fajardy and Mac Dowell 2017; Tanzer 37
and Ramírez 2019). 38
Acceptability of bioenergy is relatively low compared to other renewable energy sources like solar and 39
wind (Poortinga et al. 2013; EPCC 2017; Peterson et al. 2015; Ma et al. 2015) and comparable to 40
natural gas (Scheer et al. 2013). People also know relatively little about bioenergy compared to other 41
energy sources (Whitmarsh et al. 2011a; EPCC 2017) and tend be be more ambivalent towards 42
bioenergy compared to other mitigation options (Allen and Chatterton 2013). People evaluate biomass 43
from waste products (e.g., food waste) more favourably than grown-for-purpose energy crops, which 44
are more controversial (Demski et al. 2015; Plate et al. 2010). The most pressing concerns for use of 45
