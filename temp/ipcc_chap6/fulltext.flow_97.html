long-term, low-risk revenues; and (4) changes to regulations to restructure and limit excessive returns 9
and easy entry of new retail competitors, all recognizing local and national concerns through state and 10
federal regulatory agencies. The example shows how decision-making and the infrastructures involved 11
are layered, and can create “nested hierarchies” where institutions fulfill multiple roles for energy 12
governance or regulation simultaneously (Stern et al. 2016b). Internationally and across different parts 13
of the energy system, institutional challenges such as these could become even more stark and complex 14
(Van de Graaf 2013). 15
6.6.4
Regional Circumstances and Net-zero Energy Systems
16
Countries have flexibility to pursue options that make the most sense for their national circumstances 17
(Figure 6.25). They may emphasize supply transformation over demand reduction; deploy different 18
resources; engage at different levels in international energy trade; support different energy industries; 19
focus on different energy carriers (e.g., electricity, hydrogen); or focus more on distributed or integrated 20
systems, among others. Many factors may influence the long-term net zero energy systems that are 21
appropriate for any country’s national circumstances, including the following. 22
Future Technology. Technological transitions have often been driven by the relative merits of different 23
technology options. Recent trends in the use of PV cells, wind power, and in batteries, for example, 24
have been spurred by their increasing economic competitiveness (Section 6.3). Yet future technology 25
cannot be fully predicted, so it provides only a partial guide today for charting a path toward future 26
systems. 27

Regional Climate. Climate influences heating and cooling demand, both of which influence countries’ 34
energy demands and energy infrastructure to meet those demands (Section 6.5). In addition to daily 35
demand profiles, heating and cooling are seasonal, influencing which energy sources may serve these 36
loads and the seasonal storage they require. Cooling is almost entirely served by electricity today, and 37
heating has commonly been served by non-electric fuels. In low-carbon energy systems, heating may 38
be increasingly served by electricity (Section 6.6.4), meaning that the influence of regional climate may 39
be strongest on countries’ electricity systems. 40
Current Energy System Configuration. Future sectoral energy demands and the potential for demand-41
side transformation are partially determined by existing infrastructure (e.g., building stocks, transport 42
infrastructure). Countries with less developed or growing energy systems will have more flexibility to 43
create the systems that best match their long-term goals, but there may be substantial challenges in 44
transitioning directly to the most advanced low-carbon technology options, and countries may have 45
different capacities to absorb technology from other countries. 46
