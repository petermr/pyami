6.4.4.2
Societal Dimensions of Energy Storage 7
Public awareness knowledge about electricity storage technologies, their current state, and their 8
potential role in future energy systems is limited (Jones et al. 2018). For instance, people do not perceive 9
energy system flexibility and storage as a significant issue, or assume storage is already taking place. 10
Public perceptions differ across storage technologies. Hydrogen is considered a modern and clean 11
technology, but people also have safety concerns. Moreover, the public is uncertain about hydrogen 12
storage size and the possibility of storing hydrogen in or near residential areas (Eitan and Fischhendler 13
2021). Battery storage both on the household and community level was perceived as slightly positive 14
in one study in the UK (Ambrosio-Albala et al. 2020). However, financial costs are seen as a main 15
barrier. The potential of electric vehicle batteries to function as flexible storage is limited by the current 16
numbers of EV owners and concerns that one’s car battery might not be fully loaded when needed. 17

Energy Transport and Transmission
18
The linkage between energy supply and distribution, on the one hand, and energy use on the other is 19
facilitated by various mechanisms for transporting energy. As the energy system evolves, the way that 20
energy is transported will also evolve. 21
6.4.5.1
Hydrogen: Low-Carbon Energy Fuel 22
Hydrogen is a promising energy carrier for a decarbonized world (Box 6.9). It
can be utilized for 23
electricity, heat, transport, industrial demand, and energy storage (Abdin et al. 2020). In low-carbon 24
energy systems, hydrogen is expected to be utilized in applications that are not as amenable to 25
electrification, such as a fuel for heavy-duty road transport and shipping, or as a chemical feedstock 26
(Griffiths et al. 2021; Schemme et al. 2017). Hydrogen could also provide low-carbon heat for industrial 27
processes or be utilized for direct reduction of iron ore (Vogl et al. 2018). Hydrogen could replace 28
natural gas-based electricity generation (do Sacramento et al. 2013) in certain regions and support the 29
integration of variable renewables into electricity systems by providing a means of long-term electricity 30
storage. Hydrogen-based carriers, such as ammonia and synthetic hydrocarbons, can likewise be used 31
in energy-intensive industries and the transport sector (Schemme et al. 2017; IRENA 2019b) (e.g., 32
synthetic fuels for aviation). These hydrogen-based energy carriers are easier to store than hydrogen. 33
At present hydrogen has limited applications – mainly being produced onsite for the creation of 34
methanol and ammonia (IEA 2019c), as well as in refineries. 35
Low- or zero-carbon produced hydrogen is not currently competitive for large-scale applications, but it 36
is likely to have a significant role in future energy systems, due to its wide-range of applications (high 37
confidence). Key challenges for hydrogen are: (a) cost-effective low/zero carbon production, (b) 38
delivery infrastructure cost, (c) land area (i.e., ‘footprint’) requirements of hydrogen pipelines, 39
compressor stations, and other infrastructure, (d) challenges in using existing pipeline infrastructure, 40
(e) maintaining hydrogen purity, (e) minimizing hydrogen leakage, and (f) the cost and performance of 41
end-uses. Furthermore, it is necessary to consider the public perception and social acceptance of 42
hydrogen technologies and their related infrastructure requirements (Scott and Powells 2020; Iribarren 43
et al. 2016) 44
