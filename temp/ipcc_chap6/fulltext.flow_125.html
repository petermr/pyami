The attractiveness of energy sector mitigation ultimately depends on the way that it provides benefits 21
and reduces the costs for the many different priorities that societies value(Wei et al. 2018, 2020; Yang 22
et al. 2018a). While costs and benefits of climate mitigation are often considered in the context of pure 23
economic outcomes – for example, GDP effects or changes in value of consumption – costs and benefits 24
should be viewed with a broader lens that accounts for the many ways that the energy system interacts 25
with societal priorities (Karlsson et al. 2020). Climate mitigation is not separate from countries’ broader 26
growth and development strategies, but rather as a key element of those strategies. 27
Cost reductions in key technologies, particularly in electricity and light-duty transport, have increased 28
the economic attractiveness of near-term low-carbon energy system transitions (high confidence). The 29
near-term, economic outcomes of low-carbon energy system transitions in some sectors and regions 30
may be on par with or superior to those of an emissions-intensive future (high confidence). Even in 31
cases when system costs are higher for low-carbon transitions, these transitions may still be 32
economically favourable when accounting for health impacts and other co-benefits (Gielen et al. 2019). 33
