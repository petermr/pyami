Successful mitigation must work in tandem with fundamental development goals such as access to 1
modern forms of energy. In many developing countries, access to electricity, clean cooking fuels, and 2
modern and efficient energy remain an essential societal priority. This is particularly true in sub-Saharan 3
Africa and several Asian countries. SDG7 on universal access to modern energy includes targets on 4
modern energy services, renewable energy, and energy efficiency, which implies a profound 5
transformation of the current energy systems. Although there are different definitions of energy access, 6
the ultimate goal is universal access to clean and modern fuels. 7

Despite progress in some countries such as India, Bangladesh and Kenya, 860 million people were 10
without access to electricity in 2018, compared with 1.2 billion in 2010. About 2.65 billion households 11
were cooking with solid fuels, distributed across Asia and Africa (IEA et al. 2020). Around 850 million 12
people in sub-Saharan Africa relied on traditional biomass (firewood and charcoal) for cooking, and 60 13
million relied on kerosene and coal to meet their energy needs (IEA 2018a). Air pollution was likely 14
responsible for 1.1 million deaths across Africa in 2019 (Fisher et al. 2021). It has been estimated that 15
2.2 billion people will still be dependent on inefficient and polluting energy sources for cooking by 16
2030, mainly in Asia and Sub-Saharan Africa, and 650 million people are likely to remain without 17
access to electricity in 2030, 90% of whom will reside in Sub-Saharan Africa (IEA et al. 2020). 18
Research indicates that decentralised and on-grid renewables are likely the least cost options to provide 19
universal access to electricity by 2030 (Section 6.4.2). Natural gas, LPG, and improved biomass 20
cookstoves are the most important options for cooking. Universal access to electricity and clean cooking 21
requires a rapid shift from traditional biomass to cleaner fuels and/or clean cooking technologies (IEA 22
et al. 2020). It has been estimated that the provision of electricity and clean cooking for all would require 23
USD 786 billion in cumulative investment to 2030, equal to 3.4% of total energy sector investment over 24
the period (IEA 2017). 25
