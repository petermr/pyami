6.4.2.7. Fossil Energy 29
Fossil fuels could play a role in climate change mitigation if strategically deployed with CCS (high 30
confidence). On the one hand, the primary mechanism for reducing emissions is to eliminate the 31
unabated fossil fuel use. On the other hand, fossil energy combined with CCS provides a means of 32
producing low-carbon energy while still utilizing the available base of fossil energy worldwide and 33
limiting stranded assets. While Section 6.4.2.5 discusses the important aspects of CCS with fossil fuels, 34
this section aims to elucidate the feasibility criteria around these fuels itself. 35
Fossil fuel reserves have continued to rise because of advanced exploration and utilization techniques 36
(high confidence). A fraction of these available reserves can be used consistent with mitigation goals 37
when paired with CCS opportunities in close geographical proximity (high confidence). Based on 38
continued exploration, the fossil fuel resource base has increased significantly; for example, a 9% 39
increase in gas reserves and 12% in oil reserves was observed in the U.S. between 2017 and 2018. This 40
increase is a result of advanced exploration techniques, which are often subsidized (Lazarus and van 41
Asselt 2018; MA et al. 2018). Fossil reserves are distributed unevenly throughout the globe. Coal 42
represents the largest remaining resource (close to 500 ZJ). Conventional oil and gas resources are an 43
order of magnitude smaller (15–20 ZJ each). Technological advances have increased the reserves of 44
unconventional fossil in the last decade. Discovered ultimate recoverable resources of unconventional 45
oil and gas are comparable to conventional oil and gas (Fizaine et al. 2017). 46
