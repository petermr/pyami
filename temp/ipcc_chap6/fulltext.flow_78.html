Box 6.6 Energy Resilience 17
In February 2021, the state of Texas was hit by three major storms and suffered significant scale power 18
outages. More than 4.5 million homes and businesses on the Texas electric grid were left without 19
electricity for days, limiting the ability to heat homes during dangerously low temperatures and leading 20
to food and clean water shortages (Busby et al. 2021). The Texas and other events – e.g., during 21
Typhoon Haiyan that affected Southeast Asia in 2013; the Australian bush fires in 2019–2020 and forest 22
fires in 2018 in California; water shortages in Cape Town, South Africa in 2018 and the western United 23
States during 2021 – raise the question of whether future low-carbon energy systems will be more or 24
less resilient than those of today. 25
Some characteristics of low-carbon energy systems will make them less resilient. Droughts reduce 26
hydroelectric electricity generation (Gleick 2016; van Vliet et al. 2016c); wind farms do not produce 27
electricity in calm conditions or shut down in very strong winds (Petersen and Troen 2012); solar PV 28
generation is reduced by clouds and is less efficient under extreme heat, dust storms, and wildfires 29
(Perry and Troccoli 2015; Jackson and Gunda 2021). In addition, the electrification of heating will 30
increase the weather dependence of electricity consumption (Staffell and Pfenninger 2018; Gea-31
Bermúdez et al. 2021). Non-renewable generation, for example from nuclear and fossil power plants, 32
are also vulnerable to high temperatures and droughts as they depend on water for cooling (Cronin et 33
al. 2018; Ahmad 2021). 34
But some aspects of low-carbon energy systems will make them more resilient. Wind and solar farms 35
are often spread geographically, which reduces the chances of being affected by the same extreme 36
weather event (Perera et al. 2020). The diversification of energy sources, in which each component has 37
different vulnerabilities, increases resilience. Less reliance on thermal electricity generation 38
technologies will reduce the risks of curtailment or efficiency losses from droughts and heat waves. 39
(Lohrmann et al. 2019). More generally, increased electricity system integration and flexibility (Section 40
6.4.3) and weatherization of generators increases electricity system resilience (Heffron et al. 2021; 41
Busby et al. 2021). Likewise, local district micro-grids with appropriate enabling technologies (e.g., 42
distributed generation, energy storage, greater demand-side participation, electric vehicles) may ensure 43
access to electricity during major long-duration power outage events and radically enhance the 44
resilience of supply of essential demand (Stout et al. 2019). 45
