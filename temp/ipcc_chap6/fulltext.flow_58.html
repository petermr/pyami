Thermal Energy Storage (TES). Thermal energy storage refers to a range of technologies exploiting the 6
ability of materials to absorb and store heat or cold, either within the same phase (sensible TES), through 7
phase changes (latent TES), or through reversible chemical reactions (thermochemical TES). Pumped 8
Thermal Energy Storage (PTES), a hybrid form of TES, is an air-driven electricity storage technology 9
storing both heat and cold in gravel beds, using a reversible heat-pump system to maintain the 10
temperature difference between the two beds and gas compression to generate and transfer heat (Regen 11
2017). TES technologies can store both heat and cold energy for long periods, for example in 12
underground water reservoirs for balancing between seasons (Tian et al. 2019; Dahash et al. 2019), 13
storing heat and cold to balance daily and seasonal temperatures in buildings and reducing heat build-14
up in applications generating excessive waste heat, such as data centres and underground operations. 15
TES can be much cheaper than batteries and has the unique ability to capture and reuse waste heat and 16
cold, enabling the efficiency of many industrial, buildings, and domestic processes to be greatly 17
improved (high confidence). Integration of TES into energy systems is particularly important, as the 18
global demand for cooling is expected to grow (Peters and Sievert 2016; Elzinga et al. 2014). Sensible 19
TES is well developed and widely used; latent TES is less developed with few applications. 20
Thermochemical TES is the least developed, with no application yet (Prieto et al. 2016; Clark et al. 21
2020). The potential for high-density storage of industrial heat for long periods in thermochemical TES 22
(Brandon et al. 2015) is high, with energy densities comparable to that of batteries (Taylor et al. 2012), 23
but material costs are currently prohibitive, ranging from hundreds to thousands of dollars per tonne. 24
Flywheel Energy Storage (FES). Flywheels are charged by accelerating a rotor/flywheel. Energy is 25
stored in the spinning rotor’s inertia which is only decelerated by friction (minimized by magnetic 26
bearings in a vacuum), or by contact with a mechanical, electric motor. They can reach full charge very 27
rapidly, their state of charge can be easily determined (Amiryar and Pullen 2017), and they operate over 28
a wide range of temperatures. While they are more expensive to install than batteries and 29
supercapacitors, they last a long time and are best suited to stationary grid storage, providing high power 30
for short periods (minutes). Flywheels can be used in vehicles, but not as the primary energy source. 31
Flywheels are a relatively mature storage technology but not widely used, despite their many advantages 32
over electrochemical storage (Dragoni 2017). Conventional flywheels require costly, high tensile 33
strength materials, but high-energy flywheels, using lightweight rotor materials, are being developed 34
(Amiryar and Pullen 2017; Hedlund et al. 2015). 35
Supercapacitors, aka Ultracapacitors or Double Layer Capacitors (Scap). Supercapacitors consist of a 36
porous separator sandwiched between two electrodes, immersed in a liquid electrolyte (Gur 2018). 37
When a voltage is applied across the electrodes, ions in the electrolyte form electric double layers at the 38
electrode surfaces, held by electrostatic forces. This structure forms a capacitor, storing electrical charge 39
(Lin et al. 2017; Brandon et al. 2015) and can operate from –40°C to 65°C. 40
Supercapacitors can supply high peaks of power very rapidly for short periods (seconds up to minutes) 41
and are able to fulfil the grid requirements for frequency regulation, but they would need to be 42
hybridized with batteries for automotive applications. Their commercial status is limited by costly 43
materials and additional power electronics required to stabilize their output (Brandon et al. 2015). 44
