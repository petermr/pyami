The impacts of climate change on the energy system can be divided into three areas: impacts on the 1
energy supply, impacts on energy consumption, and impacts on energy infrastructure. The rest of this 2
section focuses on how the future changes in climate drivers might affect the ability of the energy 3
system transformation needed to mitigate climate change. The discussion of energy infrastructure in 4
this section is limited to electric electricity system vulnerability. 5
6.5.2
Impacts on Energy Supply
6
The increased weather-dependency of future low-carbon electricity systems amplifies the possible 7
impacts of climate change (Staffell and Pfenninger 2018). However, globally climate change impacts 8
on electricity generation – including hydro, wind and solar power potentials – should not compromise 9
climate mitigation strategies (high confidence). Many of the changes in the climate system will be 10
geographically complex at the regional and local levels. Thus, regionally climate change impacts on 11
electricity generation could be significant. Climate change impacts on bioenergy potentials are more 12
uncertain because of uncertainties associated with the crop response to climate change, future water 13
availability and crop deployment. Climate change can reduce the efficiency of thermal power generation 14
and increase the risk of power plant shutdowns during droughts. The potential additional cooling water 15
needs of CCS can increase these risks. 16
6.5.2.1
Hydropower 17
The impacts of climate change on hydropower will vary by region. High latitudes in the northern 18
hemisphere are anticipated to experience increased runoff and hydropower potential. For other regions, 19
studies find both increasing and decreasing runoff and hydropower potential. Areas with decreased 20
runoff are anticipated to experience reduced hydropower production and increased water conflict among 21
different economic activities. (high confidence) 22
Hydropower production is directly related to the availability of water. Changes in runoff and its 23
seasonality and changes in temperature and precipitation intensity will influence hydro electricity 24
production (IHA 2019). In general, increased precipitation will increase water availability and 25
hydropower production. Increased precipitation intensity, however, may impact the integrity of dam 26
structures and affect power production by increasing debris accumulation and vegetation growth. 27
Additionally, increased precipitation intensity results in the silting of the reservoirs or increases the 28
amount of water spilt, resulting in erosion (Schaeffer et al. 2012; IHA 2019). Climate change will likely 29
lead to higher air temperatures, resulting in more surface evaporation, less water storage, and loss of 30
equipment efficiency (Ebinger and Vergara 2011; Fluixá-Sanmartín et al. 2018; Hock et al. 2019; 31
Mukheibir 2013). Climate change may alter the demands for water use by other sectors that often rely 32
on stored water in multi-purpose reservoirs and may therefore generate conflicts over water use. The 33
increased need for water for irrigation and/or industry can affect the availability of water for hydropower 34
generation (Solaun and Cerdá 2017; Spalding-Fecher et al. 2016). Higher temperatures increase glacier 35
melt, increasing water availability for hydropower while the glaciers exist. Changes in the timing of 36
snow and ice melt may require upgrading in storage capacity and adaptation of the hydropower plant 37
management for fully exploiting the increase in water availability. 38
