Solar Energy. Observations and model simulations have addressed whether large-scale solar PV power 5
plants can alter the local and regional climate. In rural areas at the local scale, large-scale solar PV farms 6
change the surface characteristics and affect air temperatures (Taha 2013). Measurements in rural 7
Arizona, U.S. show local nighttime temperatures 3–4°C warmer at the PV farm than surroundings 8
(Barron-Gafford et al. 2016). In contrast, measurements in urban settings show that solar PV panels on 9
roofs provide a cooling effect (Ma et al. 2017; Taha 2013). On the regional scale, modelling studies 10
suggest cooling in urban areas (0.11–0.53°C) and warming in rural areas (up to 0.27°C) (Millstein and 11
Menon 2011). Global climate model simulations show that solar panels induce regional cooling by 12
converting part of the incoming solar energy to electricity (Hu et al. 2016). However, converting the 13
generated electricity to heat in urban areas increases regional and local temperatures, compensating for 14
the cooling effect. 15
Wind Energy. Surface temperature changes in the vicinity of wind farms have been detected (Xia et al. 16
2019; Smith et al. 2013; Lee and Lundquist 2017; Takle et al. 2019) in the form of nighttime warming. 17
Data from field campaigns suggest that a “suppression of cooling” can explain the observed warming 18
(Takle et al. 2019). Regional and climate models have been used to describe the interactions between 19
turbines and the atmosphere and find minor impacts (Vautard et al. 2014). More sophisticated models 20
confirm the local warming effect of wind farms but report that the impact on the regional area is slight 21
and occasional (Wang et al. 2019d). Wind turbines alter the transport and dissipation of momentum 22
near the surface but do not directly impact the Earth's energy balance (Fischereit et al. 2021). However, 23
the secondary modifications to the energy and water exchanges have added implications for the climate 24
system (Jacobson and Archer 2012). 25
Hydropower. The potential climate impacts of hydropower concentrate on the GHG emissions from 26
organic matter decomposition when the carbon cycle is altered by the flooding of the hydroelectric 27
power plant reservoir (Ocko and Hamburg 2019), but emissions from organic matter decomposition 28
decrease over time. The darker surface of the reservoir, compared to the lighter surrounding land may 29
counterbalance part of the reduced GHG emissions by hydropower production (Wohlfahrt et al. 2021). 30
However, these impacts vary significantly among facilities due to the surrounding land properties and 31
the area inundated by the reservoir. 32
